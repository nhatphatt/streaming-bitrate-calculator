For most streamers, **1080p at 6,000–8,000 Kbps** is still the better choice in 2026 — it reaches more viewers, works within platform bitrate caps, and avoids the downscaling penalties that make 1440p look worse than expected. 1440p streaming only pays off when you have uncapped bitrate (YouTube, Kick with direct ingest), a strong encoder like AV1 or NVENC HEVC, and viewers on high-resolution monitors. Here is how the two resolutions compare across every factor that matters.

## Resolution and Pixel Count

1080p (1920×1080) contains **2,073,600 pixels** per frame. 1440p (2560×1440) contains **3,686,400 pixels** — exactly **77% more pixels** than 1080p. That gap means 1440p needs proportionally more bitrate to encode cleanly, or the extra detail becomes muddy compression artifacts that look worse than a crisp 1080p stream.

The raw resolution advantage of 1440p only materializes when:

- The viewer has a 1440p or 4K monitor
- The platform serves the stream natively at 1440p (not transcoded down)
- Your bitrate is high enough to encode those extra pixels without visible blocking

If any of those conditions fail — and for Twitch they almost always do — 1440p can look *worse* than a well-encoded 1080p stream.

## Bitrate Requirements by Resolution

This is the most practical comparison. More pixels need more bits per frame to avoid artifacts. The table below uses H.264 (the most common streaming codec) as the baseline, with x264 medium or NVENC at quality level 18–20.

| Resolution | Frame Rate | Minimum Bitrate | Recommended Bitrate | Excellent Quality |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1080p | 30 fps | 3,500 Kbps | 5,000–6,000 Kbps | 8,000 Kbps |
| 1080p | 60 fps | 4,500 Kbps | 6,000–8,000 Kbps | 10,000 Kbps |
| 1440p | 30 fps | 6,000 Kbps | 9,000–12,000 Kbps | 16,000 Kbps |
| 1440p | 60 fps | 8,000 Kbps | 12,000–16,000 Kbps | 20,000+ Kbps |

The "minimum" values are where you stop seeing obvious blocking artifacts on static scenes. They are not comfortable for fast-moving content. If you want to stream 1440p 60fps at a quality level that actually beats 1080p, you need **12,000–16,000 Kbps** — two to three times the Twitch hard cap of 6,000–8,000 Kbps.

## Platform Bitrate Caps and 1440p Support

Platform limits are the single biggest reason 1440p streaming struggles. Most live platforms enforce ingest bitrate caps that were designed around 1080p.

| Platform | Bitrate Cap | Native 1440p Support | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Twitch | 6,000–8,000 Kbps | No | 1440p is downscaled to 1080p for most viewers |
| YouTube Live | 51,000 Kbps (HD) | Yes (with transcoding) | Best platform for 1440p streaming |
| Kick | 8,000–12,000 Kbps | Partial | Higher cap but no guaranteed 1440p transcode |
| Facebook Gaming | 4,000–6,000 Kbps | No | Aimed at 720p/1080p |

Twitch is the clearest case: if you stream 1440p to Twitch, it re-encodes your stream down to 1080p for nearly all viewers. You burned extra upload bandwidth and gave viewers a 1440p-to-1080p downscale artifact instead of a clean native 1080p encode. See [Twitch recommended settings](/platforms/twitch/) for the caps in detail.

YouTube is the exception. It accepts high bitrates and actually serves 1440p to viewers who select it. If YouTube is your primary platform, 1440p at 12,000–18,000 Kbps with NVENC or AV1 is a legitimate upgrade. Check [YouTube streaming settings](/platforms/youtube/) for the current ingest specs.

## The Downscaling Problem

When you stream 1440p to a platform that serves at 1080p, two encodes happen instead of one:

- Your encoder compresses 1440p to the stream bitrate (already lossy)
- The platform re-encodes that lossy 1440p signal down to 1080p (second lossy pass)

Each generation of lossy encoding adds artifacts. Detail that was already compressed gets re-compressed at a different resolution, and the spatial downscaling filter introduces its own softness. The result is almost always **visually inferior** to streaming 1080p natively at the same bitrate.

The only way to avoid this is to encode at exactly the resolution the platform serves — which for Twitch means 1080p, and for YouTube means 1440p or 1080p depending on what the viewer selects.

## Upload Bandwidth: Real Numbers

Bitrate is only half the picture. You also need headroom above your stream bitrate so your connection stays stable. The standard recommendation is 1.5–2x your stream bitrate as your minimum upload speed.

| Stream Setup | Stream Bitrate | Minimum Upload Speed | Comfortable Upload Speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1080p 30fps (Twitch) | 5,000 Kbps | 7.5 Mbps | 10 Mbps |
| 1080p 60fps (Twitch) | 6,000 Kbps | 9 Mbps | 12 Mbps |
| 1440p 60fps (YouTube) | 12,000 Kbps | 18 Mbps | 24 Mbps |
| 1440p 60fps (YouTube, AV1) | 8,000 Kbps | 12 Mbps | 16 Mbps |

Check your current upload headroom with the [Bandwidth Calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) before committing to 1440p. An unstable 1440p stream with dropped frames will always look worse than a rock-solid 1080p one.

## Does 1440p Actually Look Better to Viewers?

Only under specific conditions. A viewer must:

- Be on a display that is 1440p or larger (roughly 35–40% of desktop gamers in 2026, per Steam hardware surveys)
- Be watching on a platform that serves the 1440p stream natively
- Be on a connection fast enough to buffer the higher bitrate stream without downgrading

On Twitch, where the stream is transcoded to 1080p anyway, your 1440p source gives zero visual benefit to viewers. On YouTube, the benefit is real but modest — the content type matters more. Talking-head video, text-heavy UI, and productivity software benefit most from 1440p. Fast-paced shooters, dark scenes, and particle effects are bottlenecked by bitrate far more than pixel count.

## Codec Impact: H.264 vs HEVC vs AV1

The codec choice dramatically changes the bitrate math. HEVC (H.265) delivers equivalent quality to H.264 at roughly **40% lower bitrate**. AV1 is another 20–30% more efficient than HEVC.

| Codec | 1440p 60fps target quality | Equivalent H.264 bitrate |
|---|---|---|
| H.264 (x264 / NVENC) | 14,000–18,000 Kbps | baseline |
| HEVC (x265 / NVENC H.265) | 8,000–11,000 Kbps | saves ~40% |
| AV1 (NVENC AV1 / AV1 x86) | 6,000–8,000 Kbps | saves ~55% |

AV1 NVENC on an RTX 4000-series or newer can stream 1440p 60fps at 8,000 Kbps with quality that genuinely rivals H.264 at 16,000 Kbps. This is what makes 1440p viable today on YouTube and Kick if your GPU supports it. See the [codec comparison](/compare/) for a deep-dive on H.264 vs HEVC vs AV1.

## When to Choose 1440p Streaming

1440p is worth choosing when:

- **Platform is YouTube** and you target viewers on larger displays
- **GPU supports AV1 NVENC** (RTX 4060 or newer) so bitrate stays manageable
- **Upload speed is 20+ Mbps** with consistent headroom
- **Content is UI-heavy** (strategy games, coding streams, productivity software) where fine text benefits from resolution
- **You record locally at 1440p** and want the archive to match the stream quality

## When to Stick with 1080p

1080p is the right choice when:

- **Primary platform is Twitch** — 1440p is transcoded away and costs you upload bandwidth for nothing
- **Upload speed is under 15 Mbps** — the headroom for stable 1440p streaming is not there
- **Content is fast-paced** (FPS, racing, fighting games) — bitrate availability matters more than pixel count
- **GPU is older than RTX 3000-series** — H.264-only encoders need much higher bitrate for clean 1440p
- **Audience is on consoles or mobile** — these displays top out at 1080p in most cases

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I stream 1440p on Twitch?

Yes, but Twitch will transcode your 1440p stream to 1080p for the vast majority of viewers. You will spend more upload bandwidth and get no visual benefit for your audience. Unless you have Partner status with transcoding options enabled, streaming natively at 1080p on Twitch produces better results than streaming 1440p.

### What bitrate do I need for 1440p 60fps streaming?

With H.264, you need at least 12,000 Kbps for acceptable quality at 1440p 60fps, and 16,000–18,000 Kbps for genuinely sharp results. If your encoder supports AV1 (RTX 4000-series or Intel Arc), you can achieve comparable quality at 6,000–8,000 Kbps. Check your upload headroom with the [Bandwidth Calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/).

### Does 1440p look better than 1080p when streaming fast games?

Not reliably. For fast-paced content like FPS games, the encoder is constantly challenged by motion blur and scene complexity. At equal bitrate, a 1080p stream allocates more bits per pixel and typically looks sharper and less blocky than a 1440p stream. Resolution helps most for slow-paced or detail-rich content where compression artifacts are easy to see.

### Should I record at 1440p even if I stream at 1080p?

Yes, this is a common and sensible workflow. Record locally at 1440p (or even 4K) for high-quality VOD archives and YouTube uploads, while streaming at 1080p within platform caps. OBS supports separate encoding paths for stream and recording outputs, so there is no reason both have to match.

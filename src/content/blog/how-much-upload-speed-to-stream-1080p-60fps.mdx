To stream 1080p at 60fps you need a minimum of **8–10 Mbps upload speed** in practice — enough to push a 6,000 Kbps video bitrate plus 160 Kbps audio, with the 25–40% headroom buffer that prevents dropped frames. If you want to stream at YouTube's recommended 10,000–12,000 Kbps, budget **15–18 Mbps** upload. The golden rule: your total stream bitrate should never exceed 75% of your measured upload speed.

## Why Upload Speed and Bitrate Are Not the Same Number

Your internet plan advertises upload speed in Mbps, but OBS and streaming software set bitrate in Kbps. Converting is simple: 1 Mbps = 1,000 Kbps. A 10 Mbps upload line can theoretically carry 10,000 Kbps — but ISP upload speeds fluctuate, other devices on your network compete for bandwidth, and TCP overhead eats into headroom. Streaming at 100% of your tested speed guarantees dropped frames during any momentary dip.

The 75% rule is the safest baseline. For a live gaming stream where you cannot afford stutters, some streamers drop that to 65% to account for UDP packet loss and peak home network traffic.

## How to Calculate Your Own Required Upload Speed

The formula is straightforward:

- **Step 1** — Add your video bitrate + audio bitrate to get total stream bitrate (Kbps)
- **Step 2** — Divide by 1,000 to convert to Mbps
- **Step 3** — Divide by 0.75 to get minimum safe upload Mbps

Example: streaming Twitch at 6,000 Kbps video + 160 Kbps audio = 6,160 Kbps total. Divide by 1,000 = 6.16 Mbps. Divide by 0.75 = **8.2 Mbps minimum upload needed**.

Always run a speed test during peak hours — evening residential upload speeds can be 30–50% lower than the advertised rate on cable and DSL connections. Fiber connections are far more consistent. Run three tests at different times and use the lowest result as your planning baseline.

## Encoder Efficiency: The Same Upload Speed Goes Further with Better Codecs

Your encoder choice changes how much quality you get from a fixed bitrate budget — and therefore how much upload speed you actually need.

**H.264 / AVC** remains the universal baseline. It is what most viewers and platforms support reliably. At 6,000 Kbps it produces good 1080p 60fps quality. It requires the most bandwidth for a given quality level.

**NVENC H.264** (NVIDIA GPU) matches x264 Medium quality at the same bitrate with far less CPU load. Good choice for single-PC setups.

**AV1** (RTX 40-series, Intel Arc, AMD RX 7000 series) delivers roughly 25–35% better visual quality at the same bitrate compared to H.264. If your GPU supports AV1 hardware encoding and your platform supports receiving it (Twitch and YouTube both do), you can achieve excellent 1080p 60fps at only 4,500–5,000 Kbps — reducing upload requirements by around 20%.

## What If You Are Right on the Edge?

If your measured upload speed is borderline — say, 9 Mbps trying to push a 6,000 Kbps Twitch stream — here are concrete ways to stabilize your stream:

- **Use CBR (constant bitrate)**, not VBR. CBR produces a predictable upload load so your router can handle it without burst spikes. See [CBR vs VBR for streaming](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/) for details.
- **Wire up with Ethernet.** Wi-Fi adds jitter and unpredictable packet loss. A wired connection alone can eliminate most dropped-frame problems.
- **Set OBS network buffer** — in OBS, enable "Dynamically change bitrate to manage congestion" under Advanced Output to let the encoder absorb short dips gracefully.
- **Reduce audio to 128 Kbps.** Dropping audio from 160 to 128 Kbps saves 32 Kbps — small, but every bit counts at the margin.
- **Upgrade to a lower-bitrate codec.** AV1 at 4,500 Kbps on a 9 Mbps line leaves a much healthier 53% headroom vs H.264 at 6,000 Kbps.

If your upload speed is consistently under 7 Mbps, streaming 1080p 60fps will be unstable regardless of your setup. Drop to 720p 60fps at 4,500–5,000 Kbps (6–7 Mbps upload needed) or 1080p 30fps at 4,500 Kbps until you can improve your connection. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is 10 Mbps upload enough for 1080p 60fps streaming on Twitch?

Yes — 10 Mbps is the comfortable sweet spot for Twitch 1080p 60fps at 6,000 Kbps. You have roughly 33% headroom above the stream bitrate, which is enough for background traffic and ISP fluctuations. If your 10 Mbps is from a cable or DSL connection that dips during peak evening hours, wire up via Ethernet and test at night before relying on it.

### Does my download speed matter for streaming?

Download speed has no direct effect on your outgoing stream quality. What matters entirely is upload speed. However, if you are also playing an online game while streaming, download speed affects your in-game connection quality, which can indirectly cause frame drops if your router becomes congested and prioritizes download over upload.

### Why do I still get dropped frames if my upload speed is fast enough?

Dropped frames despite adequate upload speed usually point to CPU or GPU bottlenecks causing OBS to miss frames before they even reach the network, a misconfigured encoder preset (x264 "Ultrafast" produces much larger frames than "Medium"), or local network congestion from Wi-Fi interference or a shared router. Check OBS stats: if "Encoding lag" is high, the problem is compute, not bandwidth. If "Network" dropped frames are high, it is genuinely an upload issue.

### How do I check if my upload speed is stable enough to stream?

Run speed tests at [fast.com](https://fast.com) or [speedtest.net](https://speedtest.net) three to five times over an hour during peak evening hours — not at 3am. Look at the lowest result, not the average. For streaming stability, also run a ping test to your nearest Twitch or YouTube ingest server: jitter under 10ms and packet loss under 0.1% are the targets.

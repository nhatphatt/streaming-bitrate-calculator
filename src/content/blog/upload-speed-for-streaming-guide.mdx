Your upload speed sets a hard ceiling on the bitrate you can stream reliably. Pick a bitrate too close to that ceiling and your stream will drop frames, buffer, and stutter for viewers — even if your download speed looks great.

## Quick Answer

**Reserve about 25–30% of your upload headroom.** Take your real upload speed, multiply your target video bitrate by roughly 1.3–1.5x, and make sure your connection can sustain that number. For a standard 6,000 Kbps stream, that means you want around 9–10 Mbps of stable upload.

## Why upload speed matters more than download

When you stream, your PC is *sending* video out to a platform's [ingest server](/glossary/ingest-server/), so it's your upload lane that gets saturated — not the download lane most internet plans advertise. Many home connections are asymmetrical, offering 300 Mbps down but only 10–20 Mbps up. That upload figure is the only one that matters for going live.

If your encoder tries to push more data than your upload can carry, the excess gets queued and eventually dropped. In OBS you'll see this as dropped frames and a red "network congestion" indicator. Viewers see freezing, pixelation, and audio that falls out of sync. Learn how to diagnose it in our guide to [fixing dropped frames in OBS](/blog/how-to-fix-dropped-frames-in-obs/).

## The 70–75% rule (and why headroom matters)

A reliable rule of thumb: your total outgoing stream data should stay under **70–75%** of your real, sustained upload speed.

Why leave a quarter of your bandwidth idle? Three reasons:

- **Audio and overhead.** A 160 Kbps audio track plus RTMP packet overhead adds a few hundred Kbps on top of your video bitrate.
- **Network fluctuation.** Home upload speeds are rarely rock-steady. Wi-Fi interference, other devices, and ISP congestion cause dips.
- **Encoder spikes.** High-motion scenes momentarily push bitrate above your target, especially with variable rate control.

So if a speed test shows 12 Mbps up, treat your usable ceiling as roughly 9 Mbps (12,000 × 0.75), which comfortably supports a 6,000 Kbps video stream with room to spare.

## Recommended upload speed by resolution

### 720p streaming

720p is the most forgiving resolution and the smart fallback when upload is tight. At 3,000–4,500 Kbps you only need about 5–7 Mbps of stable upload. On mobile hotspots or shared connections, 720p 60fps often looks cleaner than a starved 1080p stream. See the [720p streaming bitrate guide](/size/720p-streaming-bitrate/) for exact targets.

### 1080p streaming

1080p is the mainstream target. A 6,000 Kbps stream — the effective cap on Twitch — pairs well with 10+ Mbps upload. If you're on YouTube and want to push 8,000–12,000 Kbps, plan for 15–20 Mbps. Check the [1080p streaming bitrate guide](/size/1080p-streaming-bitrate/) before bumping your numbers.

### 1440p and 4K streaming

High resolutions are bandwidth-hungry. 1440p60 wants 12,000–20,000 Kbps and a genuinely fast fiber or cable plan. Native 4K60 can climb past 30,000 Kbps, which means 45 Mbps or more of dependable upload. Unless you have symmetrical fiber, most creators get a better real-world result staying at 1080p. Details live in the [4K streaming bitrate guide](/size/4k-streaming-bitrate/).

## How to test your upload speed properly

A single speed test isn't enough. Do this instead:

1. **Test wired, not Wi-Fi.** Plug into Ethernet to remove wireless variability. Wi-Fi upload can be half your wired speed under load.
2. **Test at your streaming time.** Evenings and weekends are peak-congestion windows for many ISPs. Your 8 PM upload may be far lower than your noon figure.
3. **Run it several times.** Look for the *lowest consistent* number, not the best-case peak. That low number is your real ceiling.
4. **Watch it live.** OBS Stats (View → Stats) shows dropped frames in real time. If dropped frames climb during a stream, your bitrate is too high for current conditions.

## Fixing an unstable connection

If your upload is marginal, you have several levers before you buy a faster plan:

- **Lower the bitrate first.** Dropping from 6,000 to 4,500 Kbps often eliminates dropped frames instantly.
- **Drop the frame rate.** Going 60fps → 30fps frees up a large chunk of required bitrate at the same resolution.
- **Go wired.** Ethernet is the single biggest stability upgrade for most streamers.
- **Free up the pipe.** Pause cloud backups, game updates, and other uploads while live.
- **Enable dynamic bitrate** (in OBS) so your stream auto-lowers bitrate during congestion instead of dropping frames.

## FAQ

### Is 10 Mbps upload enough to stream 1080p 60fps?

Yes, for most platforms. A 6,000 Kbps stream plus audio uses roughly 6.3 Mbps, comfortably under 10 Mbps with headroom. Push toward 8,000+ Kbps and you'll want closer to 15 Mbps.

### Does download speed affect my stream?

Only indirectly. Streaming uploads video, so download speed doesn't limit your broadcast — but if you also game online while streaming, you need enough download bandwidth for the game on top of your upload for the stream.

### Should I use Wi-Fi to stream?

Avoid it if you can. Wi-Fi upload is less consistent and prone to interference, which causes intermittent dropped frames. A wired Ethernet connection is the most reliable choice.

## Bottom Line

Start from your real, sustained upload speed, cap your total stream data at about 70–75% of it, and match your resolution and frame rate to what's left. When in doubt, run your numbers through the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) and lean toward a stable 1080p stream over an unstable higher-resolution one.

Valorant is a competitive tactical shooter where framerate is everything — players chase 240fps and beyond to keep peeker's advantage. The good news for streamers is that Valorant is one of the easiest games to encode: it's CPU-light, GPU-light, and its clean art style compresses at bitrates that would look terrible on a busier title.

## Quick Answer

**Stream Valorant at 1080p60 with NVENC (New), 5,000–6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch (8,000–10,000 on YouTube), preset P5, and a 2-second keyframe interval.** Because Valorant runs at hundreds of frames per second, the real goal is protecting your in-game FPS — so always offload encoding to NVENC and keep OBS off your CPU.

## Why Valorant Is Easy to Stream

Two things make Valorant a dream to broadcast:

1. **It's extremely light on hardware.** Valorant is built to run on modest machines, so most gaming PCs render it at 200–400fps with the CPU and GPU barely breaking a sweat. That leaves plenty of headroom for OBS.
2. **It compresses cleanly.** The stylized art style uses flat colors, clean geometry, and minimal particle clutter. Compared to Warzone or Fortnite, there's far less visual noise for the encoder to chew through, so a sharp image is achievable at lower bitrates.

The one twist is high-refresh gameplay. You'll be playing at well above 60fps, but you should still **stream at 60fps** — 120fps streams are bandwidth-hungry, poorly supported, and pointless for viewers on Twitch. Let the game run fast locally; cap OBS output at 60.

## The Real Challenge: Protecting Your Framerate

For a competitive shooter, encoder choice is about FPS preservation, not raw quality. A CPU-based x264 encode steals cycles from the game and can tank your framerate mid-clutch. NVENC runs on a dedicated block of the GPU that barely touches game rendering, so it's the clear winner here even though Valorant is CPU-light.

- Use **NVENC (New)** so your CPU stays free for input polling and game logic.
- Cap your in-game FPS a bit below your monitor's refresh rate (e.g., 237 on a 240 Hz panel) to keep GPU load steady and frametimes consistent.
- If you're serious about zero encoding impact, a [dual-PC setup](/blog/dual-pc-streaming-setup-guide/) moves OBS entirely off your gaming machine — overkill for most, but the ultimate answer for high-FPS competitive play.

## Recommended Bitrate and Resolution

Twitch caps non-partner bitrate at ~6,000 Kbps, and Valorant looks great well under that. YouTube Live transcodes server-side and accepts far higher bitrates.

### Twitch

- **1080p 60fps:** 5,000–6,000 Kbps, NVENC (New), preset P5
- **720p 60fps:** 3,500–4,500 Kbps — a smart pick if you're squeezing out every frame

### YouTube Live

- **1080p 60fps:** 8,000–10,000 Kbps
- **1440p 60fps:** 12,000–15,000 Kbps

At 1080p60, 5,000 Kbps on Twitch looks excellent; you can even hold up at 4,500 Kbps in normal gameplay. Compare against other resolutions on our [1080p 60fps bitrate page](/size/1080p-60fps/) if you're weighing 720p vs 1080p.

## Encoder Settings

### NVENC (recommended)

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 5,000–6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 8,000–10,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **Preset:** P5 (Slow) — P6 works too, since the GPU is barely loaded
- **Profile:** High
- **B-frames:** 2
- **Look-ahead / Psycho Visual Tuning:** optional; enable only if you have GPU headroom

### x264 (only if you must)

- **Preset:** Medium on an 8-core+ CPU with FPS to spare
- **Profile:** High
- **Tune:** None — do **not** use "zerolatency," it hurts quality for no benefit

Valorant is CPU-light, so a strong processor *can* run x264 Medium — but there's rarely a reason to risk framerate dips in a competitive game when NVENC already looks great. Reserve x264 for a second encoding PC. Our [NVENC vs x264](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) comparison covers the tradeoff in depth.

## In-Game Settings for Streaming

Valorant's minimal overhead means you can prioritize clarity over performance without hurting FPS meaningfully:

- **Resolution:** 1920×1080 (native)
- **Material Quality:** High
- **Texture Quality:** High
- **Detail Quality:** Medium
- **Anti-Aliasing:** MSAA 4x
- **Anisotropic Filtering:** 4x
- **Distortion:** Off (reduces visual noise the encoder wastes bitrate on)
- **Bloom:** Off (cleaner image for stream)

## Audio Settings

- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz
- **Track/Bitrate:** 160 Kbps stereo
- Competitive players run game audio loud for footsteps — mix it so viewers hear callouts and comms clearly, and add a noise gate so your mic doesn't broadcast keyboard clatter during tense rounds.

## Platform-Specific Tips

| Twitch | 720p | 60 | 3,500–4,500 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| YouTube | 1080p | 60 | 8,000–10,000 Kbps | NVENC P5 |

- **Twitch:** Stay at or below 6,000 Kbps CBR. See the [Twitch platform guide](/platforms/twitch/) for ingest and latency options — low-latency mode is great for interacting with chat during downtime between rounds.
- **YouTube:** Push higher bitrate or step up to 1440p; the clean art style stays razor-sharp and YouTube's generous ceiling handles it.
- **Kick:** Its ~8,000 Kbps cap gives comfortable headroom at 1080p60.

## Performance Checklist

1. Use NVENC to keep encoding off the CPU and game cores.
2. Cap game FPS just below monitor refresh to stabilize frametimes.
3. Close Chrome and background apps — Valorant is sensitive to CPU contention.
4. Set OBS process priority to "Above Normal" under Settings → Advanced.

## FAQ

### Should I stream Valorant at 720p for more FPS?

If you're chasing the highest possible framerate on a mid-range card, 720p60 at ~4,000 Kbps is a legitimate choice and still looks clean thanks to Valorant's simple art. On capable hardware, 1080p60 is the better default for viewers.

### Does streaming lower my Valorant FPS?

With NVENC, the impact is minimal — a few percent at most — because encoding runs on a separate GPU block. If you see larger drops, you're likely on x264 or CPU-limited by background apps; switch to NVENC and close browsers.

A capture card takes an HDMI signal from a console or a second PC and hands it to your streaming machine as a video source. This guide focuses on the criteria that actually matter so you can pick the right card for your setup instead of overpaying for specs you'll never use.

## Quick Answer

**Match the card to your source: a USB 1080p60 external card covers most console and single-camera setups, while a PCIe 4K60 card only makes sense for 4K console capture or a dedicated dual-PC rig.** Buy for the resolution and framerate you'll actually broadcast, not the highest number on the box.

## Do You Even Need a Capture Card?

You need one when the video you want to stream lives *outside* your streaming PC:

- **Console streaming** — PS5, Xbox Series X/S, or Nintendo Switch feeding your PC.
- **Dual-PC setups** — a dedicated gaming PC sends its output to a separate streaming PC. See our [dual-PC streaming setup guide](/blog/dual-pc-streaming-setup-guide/) for the full workflow.
- **Camera input** — using a DSLR or mirrorless camera with a clean HDMI out as a high-quality webcam.

You do **not** need one if you're streaming PC games from the same PC you broadcast on. OBS Game Capture reads the frames directly with no capture hardware involved.

## The Criteria That Actually Matter

### Capture resolution and framerate

This is the number that defines the card. Cards are marketed by what they hand to your PC:

- **1080p60** — the sweet spot for the vast majority of streamers. Twitch caps non-partner bitrate around 6,000 Kbps, which is built for 1080p, so most viewers never see more than this anyway.
- **4K60** — worth it only if you genuinely broadcast or record in 4K, or want a high-quality local recording while streaming a downscaled version.

Don't confuse *capture* resolution with *passthrough* resolution (more on that below). A card can pass 4K to your monitor while only capturing 1080p to your PC.

### Passthrough

Passthrough sends the source signal straight to a second monitor or TV with effectively zero added latency, so you play on a clean feed while the card captures a copy. Two things to check:

- **Passthrough resolution/refresh** — a good card passes through a higher spec than it captures (e.g. 4K120 or 4K144 passthrough while capturing 1080p60), so console players keep their high-refresh gaming experience.
- **HDR and VRR** — if you game in HDR or use variable refresh rate, confirm the card passes those through; many budget cards flatten HDR or drop VRR.

### USB vs PCIe (external vs internal)

This is the biggest structural decision. See the table below, but the short version: USB (external) for flexibility and laptops, PCIe (internal) for the lowest latency in a fixed desktop dual-PC build.

### Software and OBS compatibility

A card is only as good as its drivers. Well-known lines from **Elgato** and **AVerMedia** are popular largely because they show up cleanly as a Video Capture Device in OBS and Streamlabs with minimal fuss. Cheaper no-name cards can work but often need extra configuration or introduce audio-sync headaches. If you hit sync problems, our guide on [how to fix audio sync issues in OBS](/blog/how-to-fix-audio-sync-issues-obs/) covers the usual culprits.

### Latency

All capture adds a little delay to the captured feed — which is exactly why passthrough exists (you play on the passthrough, not the captured image). PCIe cards generally add the least latency; USB 3.0 cards are close behind; older USB 2.0 cards add the most.

## USB vs PCIe Capture Cards

| Factor | USB (External) | PCIe (Internal) |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Installation | Plug and play | Open the case, seat the card |
| Portability | Move between PCs/laptops | Fixed to one desktop |
| Latency | Low (USB 3.0) to moderate (USB 2.0) | Lowest |
| Bandwidth headroom | Capped by the USB bus | High — good for 4K60 capture |
| Best for | Consoles, laptops, single-PC captures | Dedicated dual-PC and 4K rigs |

## Card Categories by Use Case

Rather than chase specific model prices (which shift constantly), match your situation to a category:

- **Budget 1080p console capture** — a compact external USB card. Great for streaming a single console at 1080p60. Compact USB 2.0 options exist but add more latency than USB 3.0.
- **Mainstream 1080p60 external** — the workhorse category. A USB 3.0 external card with HDR passthrough handles PS5/Xbox/Switch and doubles as a camera capture. This is what most streamers should buy.
- **4K external** — a USB 3.x card that captures 4K60 without opening your case. Good for creators who want 4K recordings but value portability.
- **4K internal (PCIe)** — for dedicated dual-PC setups or 4K console capture where you want the lowest latency and highest bandwidth headroom. Look for high-refresh passthrough if you game above 60fps.

Both **Elgato** and **AVerMedia** field cards across all of these tiers, so pick the tier first and compare current models within it.

## Setting Up a Capture Card in OBS

1. Connect your console or gaming PC to the capture card's **input**.
2. Connect the capture card to your streaming PC via USB or PCIe.
3. In OBS, add a **Video Capture Device** source.
4. Select the card from the device dropdown.
5. Set the resolution/FPS to match the card's capture output (usually 1080p60).
6. Configure your stream bitrate for that output.

Since a card outputs at a fixed resolution, your encoder settings are identical to normal streaming. Use the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) to confirm your upload can carry the bitrate, and the [1080p 60fps bitrate](/size/1080p-60fps/) reference for target numbers. If your card captures 4K, check the [4K 60fps](/size/4k-60fps/) page and be sure your upload speed can sustain the much higher bitrate.

## FAQ

### Do I need a 4K capture card for a 1080p stream?

No. If you broadcast at 1080p, a 1080p60 card is the correct choice. A 4K card only helps if you capture or record in 4K locally.

### Will a capture card add input lag to my gameplay?

Not if you use passthrough. You play on the passthrough output, which is essentially lag-free; only the copy sent to your PC carries the small capture delay.

### USB or PCIe for a dual-PC setup?

Either works. PCIe gives the lowest latency and most bandwidth for a permanent desktop rig; a USB 3.0 card is fine and far easier to move or troubleshoot.

## Bottom Line

Buy for the resolution you'll actually stream. Most creators are best served by a mainstream **USB 3.0 1080p60 external card** with HDR passthrough — well-supported lines from **Elgato** and **AVerMedia** are safe picks. Step up to a **PCIe 4K60** card only for dedicated dual-PC builds or genuine 4K capture. Once your hardware is sorted, dial in your encoder with the [bitrate tools](/tools/) and platform-specific settings for [Twitch](/platforms/twitch/) or [YouTube](/platforms/youtube/).

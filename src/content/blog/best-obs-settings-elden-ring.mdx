Elden Ring is a slow-paced, atmospheric open-world RPG that streams beautifully once you understand its two quirks: a hard 60fps cap and a graphics card that's usually working hard even before OBS enters the picture. Get those right and you can hit excellent quality at very reasonable bitrates.

## Quick Answer

**Stream Elden Ring at 1080p60 with NVENC (New), 5,000–6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch (8,000–10,000 on YouTube), preset P6, a 2-second keyframe interval, and both motion blur and depth of field off.** The 60fps engine cap means there's no point setting OBS above 60 — spend that headroom on encoder quality instead.

## Why Elden Ring Compresses Efficiently

Elden Ring is GPU-heavy but encoder-friendly. Here's the difference. FromSoftware's engine leans on your graphics card for lush environments, volumetric fog, and dense foliage, which is why the game can stutter on mid-range hardware. But from a compression standpoint, it's one of the friendlier open-world titles:

- The camera is third-person and relatively controlled — no rapid 180-degree flick shots that force the encoder to redraw the whole frame.
- The art direction favors muted colors, soft atmospheric lighting, and gentle gradients, all of which compress cleanly.
- Traversal is slow. You're mostly walking, riding Torrent, or dodging at a measured pace, so frame-to-frame changes are modest.

The exceptions are boss fights and spell-heavy builds, where particle effects, flashes, and screen-filling attacks spike compression complexity. Those moments — not your average field exploration — should set your bitrate. Budget for the boss, not the bonfire.

## Recommended Bitrate and Resolution

Because the game compresses well, you don't need to max out Twitch's ~6,000 Kbps cap for a great-looking stream — but there's no harm in it during effect-heavy fights. YouTube Live transcodes server-side and accepts much higher bitrates for a crisper image.

### Twitch

- **1080p 60fps:** 5,000–6,000 Kbps, NVENC (New), preset P6
- **1080p 30fps:** 4,000–4,500 Kbps — acceptable since the game caps at 60 anyway

### YouTube Live

- **1080p 60fps:** 8,000–10,000 Kbps
- **1440p 60fps:** 12,000–15,000 Kbps

The atmospheric look means viewers rarely notice the difference between 5,000 and 6,000 Kbps in the open world, but the higher figure keeps particle-heavy bosses clean.

## Encoder Settings

### NVENC (recommended)

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 5,000–6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 8,000–10,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **Preset:** P6 (Slower) — the game is GPU-bound, but a P6 encode adds little extra GPU cost on modern cards
- **Profile:** High
- **B-frames:** 2

Elden Ring is a case where **NVENC is almost always the right call**, because the GPU load is high but consistent, and NVENC's dedicated encoder block barely touches your game framerate. Pushing x264 on a single PC risks CPU contention during boss fights when the engine is already busy with physics and effects. If you want to understand the tradeoff, our [NVENC vs x264](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) breakdown covers when each wins. On an RTX 40-series card streaming to YouTube, [AV1 via NVENC](/blog/nvenc-av1-explained/) gives you noticeably cleaner fog and particles at the same bitrate.

## In-Game Settings for a Clean Stream

Elden Ring exposes a limited set of graphics options, but the two that matter most for stream clarity are motion blur and depth of field — both add smear that compresses badly and hides detail.

- **Texture Quality:** High or Maximum
- **Antialiasing Quality:** High
- **SSAO:** Medium
- **Depth of Field:** Off (cleaner, sharper image)
- **Motion Blur:** Off (critical for stream quality)
- **Shadow Quality:** Medium
- **Lighting Quality:** High
- **Effects Quality:** Medium
- **Volumetric Quality:** Medium (a common stutter culprit)
- **Grass Quality:** Medium

**Do not set OBS to 120fps.** The game is hard-capped at 60fps, so a higher OBS framerate just duplicates frames and wastes encoder cycles.

## Performance and Frame-Pacing Tips

Elden Ring is notorious for frame-pacing hitches, which look worse on a recording than they do live:

1. Cap the game to 60fps with **RTSS (RivaTuner Statistics Server)** for smoother, more consistent frame delivery than the in-engine cap.
2. Disable the Steam overlay — it's a known source of stutter in this title.
3. If the open world hitches, lower Grass Quality and Volumetric Quality first; they're the heaviest options.
4. Use NVENC so your CPU stays free for the engine's background asset streaming. If you still hit warnings, our guide on [fixing encoding overload](/blog/how-to-fix-encoding-overloaded-obs/) walks through the fixes.

## Audio Settings

- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz
- **Track/Bitrate:** 160 Kbps stereo
- Elden Ring's dynamic range is wide — quiet exploration, then loud boss music. Apply gentle compression to your desktop audio track so a sudden boss intro doesn't blow out your mic mix.

## FAQ

### Do I need x264 for Elden Ring?

No. Because the game is GPU-bound and compresses efficiently, NVENC P6 delivers excellent quality without stealing CPU cycles the engine needs. x264 only makes sense on a two-PC setup where a dedicated encoding machine has spare headroom.

### Why does my Elden Ring stream stutter during boss fights?

Two likely causes: the game's own frame-pacing hitches (cap with RTSS and disable the Steam overlay) or encoder overload from particle-heavy attacks. If it's the encoder, confirm you're on NVENC and drop the preset from P7 to P6. See our [dropped frames guide](/blog/how-to-fix-dropped-frames-in-obs/) for network-side stutter.

## Bottom Line

Elden Ring is GPU-heavy but compresses cleanly, so NVENC P6 at 5,000–6,000 Kbps on Twitch (higher on YouTube) hits a great quality-to-effort ratio — just cap the framerate at 60 and kill motion blur.

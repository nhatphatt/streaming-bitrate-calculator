League of Legends is one of the friendliest games to stream. Its fixed camera, stylized art, and modest hardware demands mean you can hit excellent stream quality without a top-tier PC or a huge upload budget.

## Quick Answer

**Stream LoL at 1080p 60fps with 4,500–6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch, or 6,000–9,000 Kbps on YouTube. Use NVENC (or x264 Medium — LoL leaves plenty of CPU free), keyframe interval 2 seconds, and High profile.** Because the game compresses so well, you rarely need to touch Twitch's 6,000 Kbps ceiling.

## Recommended Resolution and FPS

LoL is a fast-clicking game where information matters more than cinematic detail, but the isometric camera keeps motion predictable. That makes 60fps worthwhile — smooth minion movement and ability animations read better in fights — without punishing your encoder.

- **1080p 60fps** — the sweet spot for most streamers. Sharp UI text, clean champion outlines.
- **1080p 30fps** — perfectly acceptable for LoL. The fixed camera hides the lower frame rate well and frees up bitrate for image quality.
- **720p 60fps** — a good fallback on limited upload speeds; LoL's clean edges upscale nicely on the viewer side.
- **1440p 60fps** — only worth it on YouTube, where higher bitrates are available. Twitch's cap makes 1440p look softer than a well-tuned 1080p feed.

If you're unsure your connection can sustain a given tier, run the numbers through the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) before going live.

## Why LoL Is Easy on Your Encoder

Understanding why League compresses so well tells you where you can save resources:

- **Fixed isometric camera** — the viewpoint never whips around like a first-person shooter, so consecutive frames share most of their pixels. That's ideal for [inter-frame compression](/glossary/keyframe/).
- **Stylized, clean textures** — flat lighting and readable art mean little of the high-frequency visual noise that eats bitrate.
- **Predictable complexity** — laning phase is nearly static; only 5v5 teamfights with stacked ability VFX briefly spike scene complexity.

At 1080p 60fps, 4,500 Kbps looks excellent during laning. Bumping to 6,000 Kbps just gives you headroom for a Karthus ult over a Yasuo tornado in a chaotic baron fight — the only moments the encoder truly works hard.

## Encoder Choice: NVENC vs x264

Because LoL is light on both CPU and GPU, you have a rare luxury: either encoder works well.

### NVENC (recommended)

Modern NVIDIA NVENC produces quality that rivals x264 Medium while offloading the work to a dedicated chip on your GPU. Since LoL barely touches your GPU, there's ample headroom.

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 4,500–6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 6,000–9,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Preset:** P5 (Quality) — go P6/P7 if your GPU has spare capacity
- **Profile:** High
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds (required by Twitch/YouTube)
- **B-frames:** 2

### x264

Unlike CPU-heavy shooters, LoL leaves your processor mostly idle, so software encoding is genuinely viable and can squeeze slightly more quality out of a fixed bitrate.

- **Preset:** Medium, or Slow on a strong multi-core CPU
- **Profile:** High
- **Tune:** none
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds

If you're weighing the two, the [NVENC vs x264 comparison](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) covers the quality-versus-overhead tradeoff in detail. On an [AV1-capable GPU](/glossary/codec/) (RTX 40-series or Arc), AV1 to YouTube or Kick can look noticeably cleaner at the same bitrate.

## In-Game Graphics Settings

LoL runs on almost anything, so unless you're on very old hardware, crank the visuals — better source frames give the encoder more to work with:

- **Graphics Quality:** Very High
- **Character / Environment / Effects Quality:** Very High
- **Anti-Aliasing:** On (clean edges compress well and reduce shimmer)
- **Frame Rate Cap:** 144 or Uncapped
- **Wait for Vertical Sync:** Off (VSync adds input latency)

The one caveat: if you also run [Streamlabs](/blog/obs-vs-streamlabs-2026/) overlays, chat widgets, and a browser, close unused tabs so nothing steals frames during a fight.

## Audio Settings

Audio quality is where many LoL streamers under-invest, yet it's cheap on bandwidth:

- **Track Bitrate:** 160 Kbps AAC (128 Kbps is fine if upload is tight)
- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz to match OBS defaults
- **Mic Filters:** Noise Suppression (RNNoise) + a Compressor to tame click-heavy keyboard sounds
- **Separate Tracks:** put mic and game on different tracks so VODs stay monetization-safe against in-game music

## Platform-Specific Tips

- **Twitch:** Stick to CBR at 6,000 Kbps max and keep keyframes at 2s, or the [ingest server](/glossary/ingest-server/) may reject the feed. Enable low-latency mode for chat interaction.
- **YouTube:** Push bitrate to 9,000+ Kbps — server-side transcoding means viewers on slow connections still get a smooth lower-quality rendition.
- **Kick:** Allows up to ~8,000 Kbps, so LoL can look pristine there without much effort.

## FAQ

### Can I stream LoL on a laptop?

Yes. LoL's low demands make it one of the best games to stream on a gaming laptop. Use NVENC at 1080p 30–60fps, cap in-game FPS to reduce heat, and keep OBS in Game Capture mode.

### Is x264 or NVENC better for League?

For most people NVENC is the smarter pick — it frees your CPU and quality is excellent. Only choose x264 Medium/Slow if you want to squeeze the last few percent of quality from a fixed bitrate and have CPU to spare.

### Why does my stream look soft during teamfights?

Ability-effect explosions momentarily overwhelm the encoder. Raise Twitch bitrate to the full 6,000 Kbps, use CBR, and confirm your keyframe interval is 2 seconds. On YouTube, simply push more bitrate.

## Bottom Line

League of Legends is the ideal "starter" streaming game — forgiving on hardware, kind to your encoder, and great-looking even at Twitch's modest cap. Set 1080p 60fps, CBR near 6,000 Kbps, NVENC P5 with 2-second keyframes, and spend the effort you saved on good audio and overlays.

Choosing between NVENC and x264 is one of the most consequential decisions in your streaming setup. It affects how sharp your stream looks, how many frames your game loses, and whether you need one PC or two. This guide breaks down the real differences and tells you exactly which one to pick.

## Quick Answer

**For most single-PC streamers in 2026, NVENC on an RTX GPU is the right choice — it delivers quality nearly identical to x264 Medium with almost no impact on game performance. Only choose x264 if you have a dedicated streaming PC or a very strong CPU paired with a weak GPU.**

## What Is NVENC?

NVENC (NVIDIA Encoder) is a hardware [encoder](/glossary/encoding/) built into NVIDIA GPUs. Crucially, it lives on a dedicated block of silicon separate from the CUDA cores that render your game. Because encoding and rendering use different parts of the chip, capturing and compressing your stream costs you only a handful of frames rather than tanking your FPS.

The quality of NVENC has jumped generationally. Pascal cards (GTX 10 series) used an older, softer encoder. Turing (RTX 20 series) introduced "NVENC New," which closed most of the gap with software encoding, and Ada (RTX 40 series) refined it further while adding AV1 support. If you have an RTX card, you already own one of the best hardware encoders available.

**Supported GPUs:** GTX 600 series and newer for H.264. RTX 20/30/40/50 series deliver the best quality ("NVENC New"). RTX 40 series and newer add hardware AV1.

## What Is x264?

x264 is a software encoder that runs on your CPU. It is the reference-grade implementation of H.264 and, at aggressive presets, produces the best quality-per-bitrate of any H.264 encoder in existence. The catch is that this quality is expensive: x264 hammers your processor, and the slower the preset, the more cores it demands.

x264 presets range from `ultrafast` to `placebo`. Streamers realistically live between `veryfast` and `slow`. Each step slower squeezes more detail out of the same bitrate but consumes more CPU. On a single gaming PC, running x264 Medium while also playing a demanding title usually causes stutter or dropped frames — which is why x264 shines most on a second, dedicated encoding machine.

## Quality and Performance Comparison

The table below assumes a 1080p 60fps stream at Twitch's 6,000 Kbps cap. Usage figures are approximate and vary by CPU, GPU, and game.

| Encoder | Quality at 6 Mbps | CPU Load | GPU Load | Best For |
|---------|-------------------|----------|----------|----------|
| NVENC (Turing/Ada+) | Excellent | ~2% | Low (dedicated block) | Most single-PC streamers |
| NVENC (Pascal/old) | Good | ~2% | Low | Budget GTX setups |
| x264 Medium | Excellent+ | High (30–60%) | 0% | Dedicated stream PC |
| x264 Slow | Outstanding | Very high (60–90%) | 0% | Dual-PC / low-motion content |
| x264 Faster | Good | Moderate (15–25%) | 0% | Strong CPU, weak GPU |
| NVENC AV1 (RTX 40+) | Best | ~2% | Low | YouTube at lower bitrates |

**Key insight:** on RTX 20 series and newer, NVENC quality is close enough to x264 Medium that most viewers cannot tell them apart on a live stream. The old advice that "x264 always looks better" comes from the Pascal era and no longer holds for modern NVIDIA cards.

## When to Use NVENC

- You have an **NVIDIA RTX GPU** (2060 or newer)
- You stream and game from a **single PC**
- You play **GPU-intensive titles** — NVENC's separate block keeps the hit small
- You want the **simplest, most reliable setup** with the fewest dropped-frame headaches

### Recommended NVENC Settings

- **Encoder:** NVENC H.264 (or AV1 on RTX 40+ for YouTube)
- **Rate Control:** [CBR](/glossary/cbr/) — required for stable live streaming
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps for Twitch; 8,000–12,000 Kbps for YouTube
- **Preset:** P6/P7 (Slower/Slowest quality) if your GPU has headroom; P5 if you notice any FPS loss
- **Profile:** High
- **Multipass:** Quarter or Full resolution for cleaner motion
- **B-frames:** 2, with a 2-second [keyframe](/glossary/keyframe/) interval

## When to Use x264

- You have a **powerful CPU** (Ryzen 7/9, Intel i7/i9) with cores to spare
- You have a **weak GPU** where even NVENC's small hit matters
- You run a **dedicated streaming PC** with a [capture card](/blog/best-capture-card-for-streaming/)
- You play **CPU-light games** like League of Legends, Minecraft, or visual novels

### Recommended x264 Settings

- **Encoder:** x264
- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 8,000–12,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Preset:** Medium for the best balance; drop to Fast if the CPU is struggling
- **Profile:** High
- **Tune:** None (a film/animation tune can help niche content but usually leave it off)

## What About AMD and Intel Encoders?

NVIDIA is not the only game in town. AMD's AMF encoder has improved dramatically — on RX 7000 series cards it lands close to NVENC, while RX 6000 and older trail noticeably. Intel's QuickSync, and especially the Arc GPUs' AV1 encoder, is a genuinely strong option for Intel-based builds. If you're weighing the AMD side specifically, see our [AMD AMF vs NVENC](/blog/amd-amf-vs-nvenc-encoding/) breakdown.

## What About AV1?

RTX 40 series (and Arc) support **hardware AV1**, which delivers roughly 30% better quality than H.264 at the same bitrate. That efficiency is most useful where you're bitrate-limited. Support varies by platform, so check our [codec comparison](/compare/) before switching. YouTube accepts AV1 ingest today; Twitch and Kick remain H.264-first for most creators, so keep an H.264 profile ready as a fallback.

## Bottom Line

For the large majority of streamers, NVENC on a modern RTX GPU is the correct answer: it's simple, high-quality, and barely touches your game's frame rate. Reserve x264 for dedicated two-PC setups or CPU-rich, GPU-poor builds where its quality-per-bitrate edge actually pays off.

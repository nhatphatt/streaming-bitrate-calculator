Rocket League is one of the cleaner-streaming games out there — flat shading, predictable physics, and clear motion vectors make it efficient to compress. But "easy to stream" doesn't mean "set it and forget it." Competitive players run at 240 Hz and demand crisp input clarity, while RLCS-style viewers want every aerial to be readable. Here's how to balance both in OBS.

## Quick Answer

**Stream Rocket League at 1080p 60fps with 6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch using NVENC preset P4 — it doesn't need P5. The clean, low-entropy scene means even a light encoder produces a sharp image. Save your expensive presets for harder games.**

Rocket League is the rare title where encoder choice and preset barely matter, which frees you to prioritize frame rate and camera comfort.

## Why Rocket League Is Encoder-Friendly

Compared to AAA shooters, Rocket League's scene is straightforward:

- **Flat colors and clean lighting** mean low entropy per frame.
- **Predictable physics-based motion** lets the encoder guess motion vectors accurately.
- **No dense particles or volumetric fog** for most of the match.

The result: a 6,000 Kbps Twitch stream of Rocket League looks closer to YouTube quality than the same bitrate on Warzone. Most viewers report Rocket League streams as clean even on modest bitrate. To see how it compares to a heavy game, our [best OBS bitrate settings](/blog/best-obs-bitrate-settings/) guide covers the general scale.

## Recommended OBS Settings by Platform

### Twitch (6,000 Kbps cap)

- **Resolution:** 1920×1080
- **FPS:** 60 (or 120 if your account has access to that ingest tier)
- **Encoder:** NVENC (New), H.264
- **Preset:** P4 (Medium) is plenty — Rocket League doesn't need P5
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps
- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **Profile:** High
- **B-frames:** 2

### YouTube Live

- **Resolution:** 1920×1080
- **FPS:** 60
- **Bitrate:** 8,000–10,000 Kbps (60fps); 12,000–15,000 Kbps if you stream at 120fps
- **Encoder:** NVENC P4 or x264 Fast (Rocket League is light enough that Fast is fine)

### Kick

- **Resolution:** 1920×1080
- **FPS:** 60
- **Bitrate:** 7,000 Kbps
- **Encoder:** NVENC P4

## In-Game Graphics for Clarity, Not Realism

Competitive players usually run low graphics for max FPS — and that also helps streaming, since fewer effects mean cleaner compression. Recommended:

- **Render Quality:** High Quality (controls resolution scale; never below 100%).
- **Render Detail:** High Performance — many top players use this.
- **Anti-Aliasing:** FXAA Medium — SMAA looks slightly better but is heavier.
- **Texture Detail:** High.
- **Particle Detail:** High Quality — Performance hides ball trails, which hurts readability.
- **Effects Intensity:** High Quality — full goal shock-waves look better on stream.
- **Light Shafts, Bloom, Lens Flares:** Off — pros disable these for clarity.
- **Dynamic Shadows:** Off — competitive standard, and it helps compression.
- **Motion Blur:** Off, always.

## Frame Rate: 60, 120, or 240?

Rocket League is broadcast at 60fps in RLCS, and streaming at 60fps is right for 99% of creators. Higher rates only matter if:

- You're competitive and want viewers to see your exact frame rate.
- Your platform supports 120fps ingest (YouTube does; Twitch only via certain partners).
- Your audience watches on 120Hz+ panels (most don't).

For most creators, **stream at 60fps even if you play at 240**. OBS downsampling from 240 to 60 is clean, and it's better to spend the bitrate budget on image quality than extra frames. Our [30fps vs 60fps streaming](/blog/30fps-vs-60fps-streaming/) piece covers the tradeoff in depth.

## Encoder Choice

Rocket League is the rare game where encoder choice barely matters because the scene is so clean:

| Hardware | Encoder | Notes |
|----------|---------|-------|
| RTX 30/40-series | NVENC P4 | More than enough |
| GTX 16-series | NVENC | Older NVENC is fine here |
| AMD RX 6000+ | AMF H.264 High | No noticeable quality gap |
| Modern Ryzen / Intel CPU | x264 Fast | OK, slightly higher delay |

Save your P5 preset and HEVC budget for harder games. If you're comparing hardware encoders anyway, see [NVENC vs x264 for streaming](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/).

## Camera Settings That Affect Viewer Comfort

Rocket League's competitive camera (high FOV, low angle, fast speed) can make spectators motion-sick on long sessions. Widely used streamer-friendly values, close to the pro range:

- **FOV:** 110 (max competitive)
- **Camera Distance:** 270
- **Camera Height:** 110
- **Camera Angle:** -3
- **Camera Stiffness:** 0.45–0.50
- **Swivel Speed:** 6.0
- **Transition Speed:** 1.40

These translate well to viewer comfort without hurting your own play.

## Audio for Rocket League Streams

Rocket League's soundtrack and crowd ambience are part of the experience:

- **Game audio:** full music plus reduced SFX is fine; keep crowd sounds audible during goals.
- **Mic bitrate:** 128 Kbps.
- **Mic filters:** RNNoise + Compressor + Limiter at -3 dB.
- **Sample rate:** 48 kHz.
- The in-game Rocket League radio music is streamer-safe on Twitch and YouTube as long as you use the in-game version — Psyonix licenses it for broadcast.

## Ranked Play and Stream Sniping

If you stream ranked, add a **5–10 second stream delay** in OBS to prevent stream-sniping in Champ-tier and SSL lobbies. The delay barely affects viewer experience — Rocket League viewers tolerate slight lag. Set it under Settings → Advanced → Stream Delay.

## Recommended Upload Speed

For 1080p 60fps at 6,000 Kbps on Twitch, target at least **10 Mbps upload**. For YouTube at 9,000 Kbps, plan **15 Mbps**. Rocket League's efficient compression means even modest 8–12 Mbps home uploads work great.

## FAQ

### Do I need P5 or HEVC for Rocket League?

No. The scene is clean enough that NVENC P4 H.264 at 6,000 Kbps looks great. Reserve heavier presets and HEVC for particle-heavy games like Marvel Rivals or Warzone where they actually change the picture.

### Should I stream Rocket League at 120fps?

Only if you play competitively and your platform supports it. For most creators, 60fps at higher image quality beats 120fps at lower quality — most viewers can't display 120fps anyway.

### Is the in-game music safe for VODs?

Yes, the built-in Rocket League radio is licensed by Psyonix for streaming when you use the in-game version. Adding your own outside music can still trigger copyright claims.

Your internet speed — specifically your **upload speed** — is the foundation of a stable live stream. This guide covers exactly how much speed you need for every platform and resolution, and how much headroom to leave on top.

## Quick Answer

**For a reliable 1080p60 stream on Twitch or Kick, aim for at least 15 Mbps upload; for YouTube 4K, plan for 50 Mbps or more.** Your upload speed should be at least 1.5× your stream bitrate so background traffic, chat, and alerts never starve the encoder.

| Stream Quality | Bitrate | Min Upload | Recommended Upload |
|---|---|---|---|
| 720p 30fps | 2,500–4,000 Kbps | 5 Mbps | 8 Mbps |
| 720p 60fps | 3,500–5,000 Kbps | 7 Mbps | 10 Mbps |
| 1080p 30fps | 4,500–6,000 Kbps | 8 Mbps | 12 Mbps |
| 1080p 60fps | 6,000–8,000 Kbps | 10 Mbps | 15 Mbps |
| 1440p 60fps | 10,000–15,000 Kbps | 18 Mbps | 25 Mbps |
| 4K 30fps | 20,000–35,000 Kbps | 35 Mbps | 50 Mbps |
| 4K 60fps | 30,000–50,000 Kbps | 55 Mbps | 75 Mbps |

## Why Headroom Matters

The 1.5× rule exists because your stream is never the only thing using your connection. Chat, browser sources, alert overlays, cloud backups, phone updates, and other people in the house all compete for the same upload pipe. If your line runs at 100% of capacity, even a small spike causes packet loss, and packet loss is what viewers see as freezing or stutter.

Bitrate is measured in kilobits per second (Kbps) and internet speed in megabits per second (Mbps), where 1 Mbps equals 1,000 Kbps. So a 6,000 Kbps stream is 6 Mbps of data, and 1.5× headroom means you want 9–10 Mbps of dependable upload behind it.

## Upload Speed vs Download Speed

- **Upload speed** is what matters for streaming. It determines how much data you can send to the platform each second.
- **Download speed** doesn't directly affect stream quality, but you need enough for chat, alerts, browser sources, and game or patch downloads happening in the background.

Most ISPs advertise download speed prominently while upload is far lower, especially on cable and DSL. Check your real numbers at [speedtest.net](https://www.speedtest.net) or [fast.com](https://fast.com), and see what each result unlocks with the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/).

## Speed Requirements by Platform

### Twitch

Twitch caps most streamers at around 6,000 Kbps and recommends constant bitrate. You need:

- **Minimum:** 10 Mbps upload
- **Recommended:** 15 Mbps upload for headroom

Full recommended settings live on the [Twitch platform page](/platforms/twitch/).

### YouTube Live

YouTube supports much higher bitrates (up to roughly 51,000 Kbps for 4K) and transcodes for every streamer:

- **1080p 60fps:** 15 Mbps upload minimum
- **4K 60fps:** 60+ Mbps upload minimum

### Kick

Kick behaves like Twitch with an 8,000 Kbps ceiling:

- **Minimum:** 12 Mbps upload
- **Recommended:** 18 Mbps upload

## What About Multistreaming?

If you broadcast to multiple platforms at once from your own PC, you need upload speed for **every stream combined**:

- Twitch (6,000 Kbps) + YouTube (9,000 Kbps) = 15,000 Kbps total
- Recommended upload: **25+ Mbps**

Alternatively, a cloud multistreaming service re-broadcasts from its own servers, so you only send one stream and only need bandwidth for that single feed. The [multistreaming guide](/blog/how-to-multistream/) walks through both approaches.

## Wired vs WiFi

**Always use a wired Ethernet connection for streaming.** WiFi introduces:

- **Packet loss** that causes stream stuttering and dropped frames
- **Latency spikes** that produce buffering for viewers
- **Bandwidth fluctuation** so your effective speed constantly shifts

Even if a WiFi speed test reads 100 Mbps, its stability is far worse than a wired link. A steady 20 Mbps wired connection is more reliable for streaming than 100 Mbps of variable WiFi.

## How to Test Your Connection

1. Run a speed test at [speedtest.net](https://www.speedtest.net) and note your **upload** figure, testing at your usual streaming hour.
2. Use the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) to see which stream qualities that speed supports.
3. Do a 30-minute test stream and watch OBS's stats for dropped frames. If dropped frames appear, your effective upload is lower than the test suggested.

## What If My Upload Speed Is Too Low?

- **Lower your resolution.** 720p60 at 4,000 Kbps looks better than 1080p60 starved at the same bitrate.
- **Lower your bitrate.** Find the sweet spot for your line with the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/).
- **Use a more efficient codec.** HEVC or AV1, where your platform supports it, delivers better quality per bit than H.264. Compare them on the [codec comparison page](/compare/).
- **Upgrade your plan.** Many ISPs sell higher upload tiers for a small premium.
- **Switch to Ethernet.** If you're on WiFi, a wired connection alone often fixes instability.

## Connection Types and Typical Upload Speeds

| Connection Type | Typical Upload | Good for Streaming? |
|---|---|---|
| DSL | 1–10 Mbps | 720p only |
| Cable | 5–35 Mbps | 1080p, yes |
| Fiber | 100–1,000 Mbps | 4K, easily |
| 5G Home | 20–100 Mbps | 1080p–4K, variable |
| Starlink | 5–25 Mbps | 720p–1080p, variable |

Fiber's symmetric upload and download make it the gold standard. Wireless options (5G, Starlink) can be fast but fluctuate, so lean toward the lower end of their range when choosing settings.

## Bottom Line

For most streamers targeting 1080p60 on Twitch or Kick, **15 Mbps upload** is the comfortable sweet spot. On YouTube pushing 4K, you need **50+ Mbps**. Whatever your target, keep upload at 1.5× your bitrate, use Ethernet, and confirm with a real test stream before going live.

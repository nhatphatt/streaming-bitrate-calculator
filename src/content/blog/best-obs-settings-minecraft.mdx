Minecraft is one of the most-watched games on Twitch and YouTube, and its blocky art style makes it one of the easiest to encode — until you add shaders. The right settings depend heavily on whether you're running vanilla, a shader pack, Java, or Bedrock, and those differences change everything from your bitrate to your encoder choice.

## Quick Answer

**For vanilla Minecraft, stream 1080p60 at 4,500–5,000 Kbps with NVENC (New), preset P6, and a 2-second keyframe interval. With shaders (SEUS, BSL, Complementary), treat it like a AAA game: push to 6,000 Kbps on Twitch and higher on YouTube.** Java is CPU-heavy, so always prefer NVENC to keep the processor free.

## Vanilla vs Shaders: The Key Difference

This is the single most important decision for a Minecraft stream, because vanilla and shaded Minecraft are almost different games from a compression standpoint.

**Vanilla Minecraft compresses beautifully.** Block visuals mean large areas of uniform color, hard edges, and simple geometry — exactly what H.264 handles efficiently. The encoder produces sharp output at bitrates that would fall apart on a realistic game. At 1080p60, even 4,500 Kbps looks crisp.

**Shaders change the math entirely.** Packs like SEUS, BSL, and Complementary add realistic lighting, water reflections, shadows, volumetric fog, and particle effects. All of that introduces high-frequency detail and constant subtle motion, dramatically increasing compression complexity. Shaders also hammer your GPU — a machine that renders vanilla at 400fps might drop to 60–90fps with a heavy pack. So with shaders you need both **more bitrate** and **more GPU headroom**.

| Mode | Twitch bitrate | GPU load | Compression difficulty |
|------|---------------|----------|------------------------|
| Vanilla, no mods | 4,500 Kbps | Very low | Easy |
| Vanilla + optimization mods | 4,500–5,000 Kbps | Low | Easy |
| Lite shaders (BSL, Complementary) | 5,000–6,000 Kbps | Moderate | Medium |
| Heavy shaders (SEUS PTGI, ray-traced) | 6,000 Kbps | High | Hard |

## Recommended Bitrate and Resolution

Twitch caps non-partner bitrate at ~6,000 Kbps. YouTube Live transcodes server-side and accepts much higher figures — a real advantage for shader packs, where extra bitrate visibly cleans up reflections and fog.

### Twitch

- **1080p 60fps (vanilla):** 4,500–5,000 Kbps
- **1080p 60fps (shaders):** 6,000 Kbps
- **1080p 30fps:** 3,500–4,500 Kbps — fine for casual, relaxed play
- **720p 60fps:** 3,000–4,000 Kbps

### YouTube Live

- **1080p 60fps:** 6,000–9,000 Kbps
- **1440p 60fps:** 10,000–14,000 Kbps (great headroom for shader detail)

## Encoder Settings

### NVENC (recommended, especially for Java)

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 4,500–6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 6,000–9,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **Preset:** P6 (Slower) — vanilla leaves plenty of GPU headroom; drop to P5 with heavy shaders
- **Profile:** High
- **B-frames:** 2

### x264 — mind the Java CPU trap

Here's the catch that surprises new Minecraft streamers: **Java Edition is CPU-heavy**, not GPU-heavy. It runs on the Java Virtual Machine, leans on single-thread performance, and does world generation, lighting, and entity ticking on the CPU. That directly competes with x264, which also lives on the CPU.

- **Java + x264:** use Faster or Fast preset only — Medium will starve the game and cause chunk-loading lag. Better yet, use NVENC.
- **Bedrock + x264:** Medium is fine. Bedrock is written in C++, runs far lighter, and leaves CPU headroom.

If you insist on x264 for Java, cap render distance and consider optimization mods (below). Otherwise, NVENC sidesteps the whole problem. The [NVENC vs x264](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) guide explains why the CPU/GPU split matters so much here.

## Java vs Bedrock for Streaming

- **Java Edition:** CPU-intensive, mod-friendly, and the standard for shaders. Always favor NVENC so the CPU can handle world logic. With mods (Fabric/Forge), allocate 4–6 GB of RAM to the game and add performance mods like Sodium and Lithium to lift framerate before shaders even enter the picture.
- **Bedrock Edition:** C++ engine, much lighter on CPU, and happy with either NVENC or x264. It's the easier edition to stream on modest hardware, though its shader ecosystem is smaller.

## In-Game Settings for a Clean Stream

### Vanilla

- **Render Distance:** 12–16 chunks (higher adds CPU load for little stream benefit)
- **Smooth Lighting:** Maximum
- **Graphics:** Fancy (not Fabulous — it adds GPU cost with little visible gain)
- **Particles:** Decreased (cuts compression noise)
- **Clouds:** Off or Fast
- **Entity Shadows:** Off

### With Shaders

- **Render Distance:** 8–12 chunks (shaders multiply the cost of each chunk)
- **Shadow Quality:** Medium in the shader settings
- **Bloom:** Low or Off
- **Motion Blur:** Off (always — it smears and wastes bitrate)

## Audio Settings

- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz
- **Track/Bitrate:** 160 Kbps stereo
- Minecraft's music and ambient sounds are quiet; balance them well below your mic so commentary and chat interaction stay clear.

## FAQ

### Do I need 6,000 Kbps for Minecraft?

Only with shaders. Vanilla Minecraft looks sharp at 4,500–5,000 Kbps thanks to its simple, blocky visuals — pushing higher wastes upload bandwidth. Once you add realistic lighting and reflections, the extra bitrate becomes worthwhile.

### Why does my Java Minecraft stream lag when I use x264?

Java Edition and x264 both compete for CPU time, so a Medium preset can starve world generation and cause chunk-loading stutter. Switch to NVENC, lower the x264 preset to Fast, or add Sodium/Lithium performance mods. Our [encoding overload guide](/blog/how-to-fix-encoding-overloaded-obs/) has more fixes.

## Bottom Line

Minecraft is easy to stream in vanilla — 4,500–5,000 Kbps on NVENC looks great — but shaders push it into AAA territory, demanding the full 6,000 Kbps and more GPU headroom. Because Java leans on the CPU, NVENC is almost always the smart choice.

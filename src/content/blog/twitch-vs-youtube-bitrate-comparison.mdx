Twitch and YouTube treat streaming bitrate very differently, and that difference decides which resolutions each platform can realistically handle. If you've ever wondered why your 1440p stream looks crisp on YouTube but soft on Twitch, the bitrate cap is usually why. Here's how the two compare and what to set.

## Quick Answer

**Twitch effectively caps most streamers around 6,000 Kbps (partners up to ~8,000), making 1080p60 its practical ceiling. YouTube Live is far more generous — up to ~12,000 Kbps at 1080p60, ~20,000 at 1440p, and ~51,000 at 4K — so it's the better home for high-resolution streaming.**

## Bitrate Limits Compared

| Setting | Twitch | YouTube Live |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Practical bitrate cap | ~6,000 Kbps (partners ~8,000) | Very high (per resolution) |
| 1080p60 target | ~6,000 Kbps | 6,000–12,000 Kbps |
| 1440p60 target | Cramped at 6,000 | 10,000–20,000 Kbps |
| 4K60 target | Not practical | 20,000–51,000 Kbps |
| Rate control | CBR recommended | CBR recommended |
| Keyframe interval | 2 seconds | 2 seconds |

The core difference: Twitch enforces a low, consistent [bitrate](/glossary/bitrate/) cap to keep costs and latency down, while YouTube leans on heavy server-side [transcoding](/glossary/transcoding/) to accept and redistribute much higher-quality streams. That single design choice ripples through everything else — the resolution you can push, the upload speed you need, and even which encoder settings make sense. Understanding it up front saves a lot of trial and error when your stream looks worse than you expected on one platform but fine on the other.

## How Twitch Handles Bitrate

Twitch is built around a tight, predictable ingest. Non-partners are effectively capped near 6,000 Kbps, and while partners can push to ~8,000, most streamers should treat 6,000 as the ceiling.

- **Best resolution:** 1080p60 at 6,000 Kbps [CBR](/glossary/cbr/) is the standard, high-quality Twitch setup.
- **Fast-motion games:** If 1080p60 blocks up at 6,000 Kbps, drop to 720p60 — each pixel gets more data and the result often looks cleaner.
- **Keyframe interval:** Set 2 seconds. This is required, not optional.

Twitch's discipline has an upside: low latency and reliable delivery, which is a big part of why chat-driven interaction feels responsive on the platform. The trade-off is that you're working inside a narrow bitrate budget, so codec efficiency matters more here than on YouTube — an efficient AV1 encode can make 6,000 Kbps look closer to what 8,000+ Kbps would on H.264. ## How YouTube Handles Bitrate

YouTube Live accepts much higher bitrates and transcodes on its end, so viewers get multiple quality options while you send a rich source stream.

- **1080p60:** 6,000–12,000 Kbps — noticeably cleaner than Twitch's 6,000 ceiling for busy scenes.
- **1440p60:** 10,000–20,000 Kbps, comfortably supported.
- **4K60:** up to ~51,000 Kbps for creators with the upload and hardware.

That headroom makes YouTube the natural choice for [1440p](/size/1440p-streaming-bitrate/) and [4K](/size/4k-streaming-bitrate/) streaming. The catch is that higher source bitrates demand a genuinely fast, stable upload and a capable GPU encoder — sending 20,000 Kbps means nothing if your connection drops frames trying to sustain it. Because YouTube transcodes server-side, viewers on slower connections still get lower-quality renditions automatically, so you can send a high source without leaving anyone behind. ## Which Platform for Which Resolution?

- **720p / 1080p live content:** Both platforms work great. Twitch's 6,000 Kbps handles 1080p60 well, and its audience and discovery are strong for live gaming.
- **1440p:** YouTube. Twitch's cap starves 1440p60, so quality suffers.
- **4K:** YouTube only. Twitch isn't designed for it.

If you can't decide, remember it's not either/or — [multistreaming](/blog/how-to-multistream/) lets you broadcast to both at once, though you'll be limited by the lower cap when using a single encode. ## Settings That Apply to Both Platforms

Regardless of platform, these fundamentals hold:

- **Use CBR** for a steady, predictable stream. See [CBR vs VBR for streaming](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/).
- **2-second keyframe interval** on both.
- **Keep bitrate at 50–70% of your upload speed** for headroom. Check yours with the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/).
- **Encoder:** x264 (Medium) or NVENC on a modern GPU works for either platform.

## FAQ

### Why does my stream look better on YouTube than Twitch?

Because YouTube accepts a much higher bitrate. At 1080p60 you might send 6,000 Kbps to Twitch but 9,000–12,000 to YouTube, so YouTube preserves more detail in fast, complex scenes.

### Can I stream 1440p or 4K to Twitch?

You can select those resolutions, but Twitch's ~6,000 Kbps cap starves them, so they'll look soft during motion. For 1440p and 4K, YouTube is the right platform.

### Is 8,000 Kbps safe on Twitch?

Only Twitch partners can reliably use up to ~8,000 Kbps. Non-partners should stay near 6,000 Kbps to avoid transcoding and delivery issues.

### Should I use the same bitrate for both platforms if I multistream?

When you send one encode to multiple platforms, you're limited by the lowest cap in the group. Multistreaming to Twitch and YouTube from a single encoder means staying near Twitch's ~6,000 Kbps, so you won't get YouTube's full quality unless you use a service or dual-encode setup that sends each platform its own stream.

## Bottom Line

Twitch keeps things lean at ~6,000 Kbps, which makes 1080p60 its sweet spot and gives it excellent low-latency delivery. YouTube's generous caps and server-side transcoding make it the home for 1440p and 4K. Pick the platform that matches your target resolution, use CBR with a 2-second keyframe interval on either, and keep your bitrate within your upload's headroom for a smooth, clean stream.

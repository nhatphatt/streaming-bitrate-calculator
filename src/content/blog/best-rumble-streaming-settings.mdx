For most Rumble streamers, **4,000–6,000 Kbps** at 1080p 60fps using H.264/CBR gives the best balance of quality and stability. Rumble does not enforce a hard public bitrate cap below 8,000 Kbps, but ingesting above 8,000 Kbps risks encoder errors and dropped frames on their servers. Set your keyframe interval to **2 seconds**, use AAC audio at 160 Kbps, and you will cover 90% of streaming scenarios.

## Why Rumble Settings Are Different from Twitch or YouTube

Rumble's infrastructure is newer and more distributed than Twitch's, but it lacks Twitch's aggressive server-side transcoding ladder. Most Rumble viewers receive your stream at or near the bitrate you broadcast — there is no automatic quality dropdown for viewers on slow connections. This means your bitrate choice directly affects how many people can watch without buffering, so going unnecessarily high hurts your audience reach. Unlike YouTube, Rumble also does not transcode to 1440p or 4K quality tiers for most accounts, so pushing above 8,000 Kbps rarely produces a visible quality gain for viewers.

## OBS Settings for Rumble (Step by Step)

Open OBS, go to **Settings > Output** and switch to Advanced output mode. These are the exact values to enter:

**Video settings:**
- **Encoder:** NVENC H.264 (new) if you have an NVIDIA GPU, otherwise x264
- **Rate control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 5,000–6,000 Kbps for 1080p 60fps
- **Keyframe interval:** 2 seconds (critical — Rumble's ingest requires this)
- **Preset:** Quality (NVENC) or Medium (x264)
- **Profile:** High

**Canvas and output resolution (Settings > Video):**
- **Base canvas:** Match your monitor resolution (1920x1080 recommended)
- **Output resolution:** 1920x1080 for 1080p, or 1280x720 for 720p
- **FPS:** 60 (or 30 for slower content or limited upload)

**Audio settings:**
- **Codec:** AAC
- **Sample rate:** 48 kHz
- **Bitrate:** 160 Kbps (or 128 Kbps to save bandwidth)

## Choosing the Right Encoder for Rumble

### NVENC H.264 (NVIDIA GPU)

NVENC is the best choice for single-PC Rumble streamers. It offloads encoding to the GPU, leaving your CPU free for the game. On RTX 30-series and newer, NVENC H.264 at the "Quality" preset matches x264 Medium quality at the same bitrate. RTX 40-series "P6/P7" NVENC presets push even further, approaching x264 Slow quality.

### x264 (CPU)

x264 produces slightly better quality per kilobit than NVENC on older hardware, but hammers your CPU. If you have a dedicated streaming PC or a modern high-core-count CPU (Ryzen 9, Core i9), set the preset to **Medium** or **Slow**. Never go below **Fast** on a single-PC gaming setup or you will drop frames.

### AV1 (RTX 40-series / Intel Arc)

Rumble's ingest technically accepts AV1 streams, but transcoding support is inconsistent as of mid-2026. Stick with H.264 unless you have confirmed AV1 end-to-end support for your account. See the [H.264 vs H.265 vs AV1 comparison](/blog/h264-vs-h265-vs-av1/) for a full breakdown of codec efficiency differences.

## CBR vs VBR for Rumble Streaming

Always use **CBR (Constant Bitrate)** for Rumble live streams. Rumble's ingest servers expect a consistent data rate — VBR can cause buffering and encoder errors because the bitrate spikes during fast-motion scenes exceed what the ingest pipeline expects. CBR also makes it easier to predict whether your upload speed can sustain the stream. For recorded VODs that you upload later, VBR is fine. For more context on why CBR dominates for live streaming, read the [CBR vs VBR for streaming guide](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/).

## Stream Keys and Rumble RTMP Ingest Settings

Rumble uses standard RTMP for live ingest. The settings to enter in OBS under **Settings > Stream**:

- **Service:** Custom
- **Server:** `rtmp://live.rumble.com/live`
- **Stream key:** Found in your Rumble Dashboard under "Go Live"

If you experience connection instability, try the backup ingest endpoint listed in your Rumble dashboard. Rumble also supports RTMPS (encrypted RTMP) — use port 443 if your ISP or network blocks standard RTMP on port 1935.

## How Much Upload Speed Do You Need for Rumble?

A safe rule: your stream bitrate should use no more than **75% of your available upload speed**. This headroom accounts for stream overhead, audio, and any other devices on your network.

| Stream Bitrate | Minimum Upload Speed | Recommended Upload Speed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 3,000 Kbps | 4 Mbps | 5–6 Mbps |
| 5,000 Kbps | 6.7 Mbps | 8–10 Mbps |
| 6,000 Kbps | 8 Mbps | 10–12 Mbps |
| 8,000 Kbps | 10.7 Mbps | 14–16 Mbps |

Run a speed test, then cross-check your numbers with the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) for a precise Mbps requirement based on your exact bitrate.

## Audio: Do Not Neglect It

Rumble audiences notice bad audio more than slightly soft video. These settings work for the majority of streams:

- **160 Kbps AAC at 48 kHz** — standard for gaming and commentary
- **320 Kbps AAC at 48 kHz** — recommended if you play music or have high-quality mic hardware
- **128 Kbps AAC at 44.1 kHz** — acceptable minimum if you need to save bandwidth

Audio bitrate adds directly on top of your video bitrate. At 6,000 Kbps video + 160 Kbps audio, your total stream bitrate is 6,160 Kbps — factor this into your upload headroom calculation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the maximum bitrate for Rumble streaming?

Rumble does not publish a strict public bitrate cap, but **8,000 Kbps** is the practical safe ceiling for most accounts. Going above this risks ingest instability and encoder errors. For standard 1080p 60fps content, 6,000 Kbps is the sweet spot — pushing to 8,000 Kbps only makes sense for 1440p or high-detail content.

### Should I use 720p or 1080p for Rumble?

If your upload speed is reliably above 10 Mbps, go with **1080p 60fps at 5,000–6,000 Kbps**. If your upload speed is 5–8 Mbps or inconsistent, **720p 60fps at 3,500–4,500 Kbps** will produce a more stable, watchable stream. A pixelated 1080p stream looks worse than a crisp 720p one.

### Can I stream to Rumble and Twitch at the same time?

Yes. Use a multistream service or OBS with multiple RTMP outputs. In practice, Twitch's 6,000 Kbps effective limit becomes your quality ceiling for both platforms simultaneously — which is fine since both platforms handle that bitrate well. Make sure your upload speed supports the combined bitrate of both streams.

### Why does my Rumble stream look pixelated during fast motion?

Fast motion in games or IRL content requires more data per frame to encode cleanly. If you see blocking or smearing during action, either **increase your bitrate by 1,000–2,000 Kbps** or **lower your resolution/FPS**. Also check your encoder preset — NVENC on "Performance" mode sacrifices quality for speed; switch to "Quality" or "High Quality" for noticeably better results at the same bitrate. You can also verify whether your current settings are causing storage issues with the [recording time calculator](/tools/recording-time-calculator/).

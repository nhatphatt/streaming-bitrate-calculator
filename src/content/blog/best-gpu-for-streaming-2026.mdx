The best GPU for streaming in 2026 is not the one with the fastest rasterization — it is the one with the best hardware encoder. An RTX 4060 with NVENC AV1 will produce a cleaner 1080p 60fps stream at 6,000 Kbps than an RTX 3090 running the older NVENC H.264 engine. Encoding hardware has become the deciding factor, and every current-generation GPU from NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel ships with a dedicated encoder that handles streaming at near-zero CPU overhead. Your raw gaming performance matters; your encoder generation matters more.

## Why the Encoder Generation Matters More Than Raw Power

A dedicated hardware encoder (NVENC on NVIDIA, AMF on AMD, QSV on Intel) offloads the entire stream encode task from your CPU and GPU shaders. The encoder chip runs in parallel with your game, so a "weaker" GPU with a newer encoder often outstreams a more powerful GPU from the previous generation.

The three encoder tiers in 2026:

- **AV1 hardware encode** — available on RTX 40/50 series, AMD RX 7000/9000, Intel Arc. Delivers roughly 30–40% better quality per bitrate than H.264 at the same file size. At 6,000 Kbps, AV1 looks close to what H.264 needs 8,000–9,000 Kbps to achieve. See the [H.264 vs H.265 vs AV1 codec comparison](/compare/) for a full breakdown.
- **NVENC H.265 / AMF HEVC** — available on RTX 20/30 series, RX 5000/6000 series. About 20–25% more efficient than H.264. Still excellent for Twitch 1080p 60fps at 6,000 Kbps but losing ground to AV1.
- **NVENC H.264 / x264 fallback** — GTX 10/16 series and older. Functional at 6,000 Kbps but noticeably softer in fast-motion scenes compared to newer encoders.

If you are on Twitch where the bitrate cap for non-partners sits at **6,000 Kbps**, the encoder generation is the single largest lever you have on stream quality. [Check your current upload headroom](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) before deciding on a streaming bitrate.

## Best GPUs for Streaming in 2026 by Budget

### Under $250 — RTX 4060 (Best Value Pick)

The RTX 4060 remains the top streaming recommendation at this price point. It carries NVIDIA's 8th-gen NVENC with dual AV1 encode engines, supports 1080p 60fps AV1 at 6,000 Kbps with near-lossless perceived quality, and draws only 115W. The 8GB GDDR6 handles modern titles at 1080p without issue.

For Twitch streaming specifically, the 4060's AV1 encoder running at 6,000 Kbps CBR outperforms what the RTX 3080's older NVENC H.264 produces at the same bitrate. Pair it with OBS using the **AV1 NVENC (new)** encoder, **CBR**, **6,000 Kbps** for [Twitch](/platforms/twitch/) or up to **12,000 Kbps** for [YouTube Live](/platforms/youtube/). Use our [OBS settings hub](/obs/) for the exact preset configuration.

Intel Arc B580 is a credible alternative at a similar price — its AV1 encoder (QSV) is competitive and works well with OBS. The B580 is particularly strong for streamers who also edit, thanks to its media engine.

### $250–$450 — RTX 4070 / RX 9070

The RTX 4070 adds enough shader performance to handle 1440p gaming alongside streaming without frame dips. The NVENC AV1 encoder is identical in quality to the 4060 — dual encode engines, same 8th-gen chip. The value here is headroom: you can game at 1440p and stream 1080p 60fps AV1 simultaneously with no perceptible impact on either.

The AMD RX 9070 launched in early 2026 with AMF AV1 encoding that finally matches NVENC quality for streaming at 6,000–8,000 Kbps. AMD's AMF encoder lagged NVENC for years, but the RDNA 4 generation closed that gap. If you prefer AMD's ecosystem or are on a Ryzen system, the RX 9070 is now a legitimate streaming card.

### $450–$700 — RTX 4070 Ti Super / RX 9070 XT

At this tier, raw gaming power catches up. Both cards run 1440p 60fps or 4K 30fps in most titles. For streaming, the encoder quality is the same as the base 4070 / 9070 — the gain is gaming headroom for 1440p or 4K output while simultaneously streaming at 1080p 60fps or 1440p 60fps.

If you plan to stream at **1440p 60fps** (YouTube supports this; [Kick](/platforms/kick/) supports up to 8,000 Kbps), the RTX 4070 Ti Super can game at 1440p and encode a 1440p stream in a single-PC setup. The required bitrate for clean 1440p 60fps AV1 is around **8,000–12,000 Kbps**. Verify your upload speed handles this with the [Streaming Bitrate & Storage Calculator](/).

### $700+ — RTX 5080 / RTX 5090

The RTX 50 series (Blackwell) ships with NVIDIA's 9th-gen NVENC, which adds multi-pass AV1 encode. At 1080p 60fps 6,000 Kbps the improvement over 8th-gen is modest (~5–8%) — these cards are overkill for streaming alone. The justification is 4K 120fps gaming headroom while the encoder handles a 4K 60fps stream at 20,000–30,000 Kbps for YouTube 4K Live.

For pure streaming value, the RTX 5080 at $700+ is harder to justify when the RTX 4060 does the same encoder job at 1080p.

## Single-PC vs Dual-PC Streaming: Does it Change the GPU Pick?

On a **single-PC setup**, the GPU handles both game rendering and stream encoding. The encoder's dedicated hardware means encoding load is near-zero on gaming performance — the 4060 and above handle this without any meaningful fps drop in most titles.

On a **dual-PC setup**, the streaming PC only needs enough GPU to run OBS and an encoder. An old GTX 1660 Super (NVENC H.264) or an Intel Arc A380 (QSV AV1, ~$100) works well as a dedicated capture and encode machine. The gaming PC's GPU selection then focuses entirely on raw rendering power.

For most streamers in 2026, a single-PC setup with an RTX 4060 or 4070 is the practical sweet spot. Dual-PC setups add cable management, latency from capture cards, and cost — the gains matter only if you stream at the top competitive level.

## What Bitrate and Encoder Settings to Use

Once you have your GPU, the key OBS settings for streaming in 2026:

- **Encoder:** NVENC AV1 (new) on RTX 40/50; AMF AV1 on RX 9000; QSV AV1 on Arc. Fall back to H.265/HEVC if your platform does not support AV1.
- **Rate control:** CBR for all live streaming. See [CBR vs VBR for streaming](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/) for why VBR causes dropped frames.
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps for Twitch (cap); 6,000–12,000 Kbps for YouTube 1080p 60fps; 8,000 Kbps for Kick.
- **Preset:** P5 (balanced) or P6 (higher quality, minimal perf impact on modern GPUs). Avoid P7 on single-PC — it adds encoder latency.
- **Keyframe interval:** 2 seconds for Twitch and Kick; 2 seconds for YouTube.

For the full recommended settings map broken down by platform, see the [OBS settings hub](/obs/) and the [best OBS bitrate settings guide](/blog/best-obs-bitrate-settings/).

AV1 is now supported by Twitch, YouTube Live, and Kick. If your GPU has AV1 hardware encode, use it — there is no reason to use H.264 for live streaming anymore on current-gen hardware.

## Does VRAM Affect Streaming Quality?

VRAM affects game rendering, not stream encoding. The encoder chip reads a copy of the finished frame, not the full VRAM buffer. An 8GB card streams identically to a 16GB card at the same resolution and bitrate — you only notice VRAM limits in-game (texture pop-in, lower LODs).

The only scenario where VRAM indirectly hurts streaming is if low VRAM causes the game to stutter, which then shows up as encode hitches. For 1080p gaming alongside 1080p 60fps streaming, 8GB VRAM is sufficient in 2026. For 1440p or 4K gaming-plus-streaming, 12–16GB becomes relevant for VRAM-hungry titles.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does a better GPU improve stream quality?

A newer GPU with a better hardware encoder improves stream quality significantly at the same bitrate. An RTX 4060 with NVENC AV1 streams cleaner 1080p 60fps at 6,000 Kbps than a much more powerful RTX 3080 using the older NVENC H.264 engine. For streaming specifically, encoder generation matters more than raw rasterization performance.

### Is NVENC or AMF better for streaming in 2026?

On current-generation hardware (RTX 40/50 vs RX 9000), NVENC and AMF AV1 quality are very close — within a few percent on VMAF scores at matched bitrates. NVENC has a slight edge in low-latency streaming presets, but the RX 9070's AMF AV1 encoder is now good enough that GPU choice can be made on gaming performance and price rather than encoder quality alone.

### Can I stream 4K on a midrange GPU?

You can encode a 4K stream with any GPU that has AV1 hardware encode, but gaming at 4K simultaneously requires significant rendering power. A realistic 4K 60fps AV1 stream for YouTube needs 20,000–30,000 Kbps upstream. Use the [Streaming Bitrate & Storage Calculator](/) to confirm your internet connection supports that. Most streamers cap at 1440p 60fps as the practical quality ceiling for single-PC setups.

### Does the GPU matter if I use a dedicated streaming PC?

On a dedicated streaming PC, even low-end GPUs with modern encoders work fine. An Intel Arc A380 (~$100) running QSV AV1 produces excellent 1080p 60fps streams because the card only needs to run OBS — no game rendering required. In that case, spend your GPU budget on the gaming PC's card and pick the cheapest AV1-capable card for the stream box.

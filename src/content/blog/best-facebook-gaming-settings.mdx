Facebook Gaming caps video bitrate at **8,000 Kbps** for standard streams, but the practical sweet spot for 1080p 60fps is **6,000 Kbps** with a 2-second keyframe interval. Unlike Twitch, Facebook re-encodes your stream for viewers on slower connections, which means your source quality directly affects every transcoded tier. Nail these settings in OBS or Streamlabs and your Facebook Live Gaming stream will look sharp on both desktop and mobile.

## Facebook Gaming Bitrate Caps and Limits (2026)

Facebook Live enforces a hard cap of **8,000 Kbps** combined video bitrate for most creators. Verified gaming creators and Facebook Gaming Partners can push up to **9,000 Kbps** in some regions, but the processing pipeline can introduce artifacts above 8,000 Kbps regardless of your account tier. The audio bitrate limit is **192 Kbps** (AAC).

One critical Facebook-specific quirk: Facebook's ingest servers require a **keyframe interval of exactly 2 seconds**. Streams sent with a 3-second or auto keyframe interval frequently cause playback stutters, grey screens, or automatic stream termination. This is more strictly enforced on Facebook than on any other major platform.

## Recommended OBS Settings for Facebook Gaming

### Video Settings

Set these in OBS under Settings > Video:

- **Base Resolution:** 1920x1080
- **Output Resolution:** 1920x1080 (downscale only if your PC or upload can't keep up)
- **Downscale Filter:** Lanczos (sharpest scaling)
- **FPS:** 60 (use 30 only if your upload speed is under 6 Mbps)

### Output / Encoder Settings

Set these in OBS under Settings > Output (Streaming tab):

- **Encoder:** NVENC (new) for NVIDIA RTX, or x264 if CPU-only
- **Rate Control:** CBR — always use CBR for live streaming, not VBR
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps for 1080p60; see full table below
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 (this is non-negotiable for Facebook)
- **Preset:** Quality (NVENC) or Medium (x264)
- **Profile:** High
- **Tune:** (none for NVENC; zerolatency for x264 with high CPU load)

### Audio Settings

- **Audio Bitrate:** 160 Kbps (128 Kbps minimum; 192 Kbps max)
- **Sample Rate:** 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz
- **Channels:** Stereo
- **Codec:** AAC

## Keyframe Interval: Why Facebook Is Different

Every major streaming platform recommends a 2-second keyframe interval, but Facebook enforces it more aggressively than others. A keyframe interval of 2 means OBS sends a full I-frame every 2 seconds (at 60fps, that is every 120 frames). Facebook's ingest servers use these keyframes to:

- Allow viewers to join mid-stream cleanly without waiting for the next full frame
- Enable VOD scrubbing after the stream ends
- Synchronize mobile and desktop playback buffers

If you leave keyframe interval on **auto** in OBS (which defaults to 0, meaning the encoder decides), Facebook's servers often reject or degrade the stream. Always hard-set it to **2**.

AV1 delivers roughly **30% better visual quality** at the same bitrate compared to H.264 — meaning 6,000 Kbps AV1 looks comparable to 8,000 Kbps H.264. If you have the hardware, it's worth enabling. ## Upload Speed Requirements

Your upload speed needs to comfortably exceed your total stream bitrate. The standard rule is to use no more than **75% of your tested upload speed** for streaming, leaving the rest for system overhead, audio, and network variability.

- **6,000 Kbps video + 160 Kbps audio = 6,160 Kbps total** → needs ~8.2 Mbps stable upload
- **8,000 Kbps video + 160 Kbps audio = 8,160 Kbps total** → needs ~10.9 Mbps stable upload

Run a speed test at least 3–4 times at different points during the day (not just once) to get a realistic picture of your upload consistency. Use the [bandwidth calculator](/tools/bandwidth-calculator/) to verify your headroom before going live.

## CBR vs. VBR for Facebook Live

Always use **CBR (Constant Bitrate)** for Facebook Gaming, never VBR. Facebook's live ingest pipeline is built around a predictable data rate. VBR streaming causes buffer underruns on Facebook's side during low-motion scenes, leading to sudden quality drops, encoding warnings in the stream dashboard, and occasional disconnections. This is the opposite of recording locally, where [CBR vs VBR](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/) is more of a genuine tradeoff.

## Comparing Facebook Gaming to Other Platforms

Facebook's 8,000 Kbps cap puts it between Twitch (6,000 Kbps for most streamers) and YouTube (no practical cap for 1080p60). The key difference is Facebook's **mobile-first audience** — a higher percentage of your viewers will be on phones versus Twitch or YouTube, which means Facebook's transcoding quality at lower tiers matters more. Start your stream at 6,000 Kbps rather than 8,000 Kbps unless you know your audience is primarily on desktop.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What bitrate should I use for Facebook Gaming 1080p 60fps?

Use **6,000 Kbps** as your starting point for 1080p 60fps on Facebook Gaming. This is well within the 8,000 Kbps cap, produces excellent quality, and gives Facebook's transcoding pipeline clean source material for viewers on slower connections. Only push toward 8,000 Kbps if you have a rock-solid upload connection above 12 Mbps and consistently see dropped frames at 6,000 Kbps.

### Why does my Facebook stream keep disconnecting or going grey?

The most common cause is an incorrect **keyframe interval**. Set it to exactly **2 seconds** in OBS (Settings > Output > Keyframe Interval: 2). Other causes include bitrate spikes above 8,000 Kbps from VBR encoding (switch to CBR), or an unstable upload connection. Check your OBS stats overlay for dropped frames — if you're dropping more than 1%, lower your bitrate by 500 Kbps.

### Should I use 30fps or 60fps for Facebook Gaming?

Use **60fps** whenever your upload speed and PC can support it. Facebook fully transcodes 60fps streams, so mobile viewers get a smooth 30fps version automatically. 60fps makes a visible difference in fast-paced games like shooters, racing games, and fighting games. Stick to 30fps only if your upload is under 6 Mbps or your CPU/GPU can't encode 1080p60 without frame drops.

### Does Facebook Gaming support 1440p or 4K streaming?

Facebook Live's ingest pipeline currently processes streams up to **1080p** for public broadcasts. While you can technically send a 1440p signal, Facebook will downscale it to 1080p on ingest. There is no benefit to streaming above 1080p to Facebook as of 2026. Save the higher resolutions for local recordings — use the [recording time calculator](/tools/recording-time-calculator/) to plan storage for high-resolution local captures alongside your Facebook stream.

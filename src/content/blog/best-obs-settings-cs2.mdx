Counter-Strike 2 is a twitch-reaction FPS where frame rate is everything. Your OBS setup has to deliver a clean stream without stealing the CPU cycles that keep your game at 300+ fps.

## Quick Answer

**Stream CS2 at 1080p 60fps with 6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch (8,000–10,000 on YouTube), using NVENC so your CPU stays free for the game. Keyframe interval 2 seconds, High profile, and cap in-game FPS well above your monitor's refresh.** In CS2 the encoder choice matters more than in almost any other game because the CPU is your bottleneck.

## Recommended Resolution and FPS

CS2 players run absurdly high in-game frame rates for input consistency, but your stream doesn't need to match. Stream at 60fps for smooth spray transfers and flick shots — 30fps makes fast crosshair movement look choppy and hides recoil control.

- **1080p 60fps** — the standard for competitive FPS streams. Sharp enough to read enemy models at range.
- **1080p 30fps** — not recommended for CS2; fast aim looks stuttery.
- **720p 60fps** — a smart fallback when upload or hardware is limited; motion stays smooth, which matters more than raw pixels in an FPS.
- **1440p 60fps** — YouTube-only territory, since Twitch's cap can't feed enough bitrate to keep 1440p sharp during smoke-filled chaos.

Prioritizing smooth motion over resolution is the core tradeoff in fast shooters. The [30fps vs 60fps streaming](/blog/30fps-vs-60fps-streaming/) breakdown explains why.

## Bitrate by Platform

CS2's Source 2 engine produces clean geometry, but smokes, molotovs, and flashbangs create sudden bursts of hard-to-compress detail. Give the encoder maximum bitrate so those moments don't smear.

| Platform | Resolution | FPS | Bitrate | Encoder |
|----------|-----------|-----|---------|---------|
| Twitch | 1080p | 60 | 5,500–6,000 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| Twitch | 720p | 60 | 4,000–4,500 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| YouTube | 1080p | 60 | 8,000–10,000 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| YouTube | 1440p | 60 | 12,000–15,000 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| Kick | 1080p | 60 | 6,500–8,000 Kbps | NVENC P5 |

On Twitch, always use the full 6,000 Kbps in CBR — the [smoke rework in CS2](/glossary/bitrate/) makes volumetric particles genuinely hard to encode, and every kilobit helps. [YouTube](/platforms/youtube/) lets you push far higher and transcodes server-side, so 8,000–10,000 Kbps keeps mid-round smokes clean. See the [twitch streaming bitrate guide](/blog/twitch-streaming-bitrate-guide/) for the full picture.

## Why CS2 Is CPU-Heavy

CS2 is one of the most CPU-bound competitive games, which directly shapes your encoder decision:

- **Sub-tick / high-frequency simulation** — the game logic, hit registration, and per-round calculations lean hard on single-thread CPU performance.
- **Huge frame rates** — pros run 300–600 fps to minimize input latency, and each of those frames is CPU work.
- **Volumetric smoke** — the new smoke grenades add particle and lighting cost that spikes during fights.

Because your CPU is already saturated feeding CS2, you should not steal it for software encoding. This is the exact scenario NVENC was built for. If you're weighing options, the [NVENC vs x264 guide](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) shows why hardware encoding wins on CPU-bound games.

## Encoder Settings

### NVENC (strongly recommended)

Offloading encoding to the GPU keeps your CPU dedicated to CS2's simulation, protecting your frame rate.

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 8,000–10,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Preset:** P5 (Quality) — P6 if your GPU has headroom (CS2 is GPU-light at competitive settings)
- **Profile:** High
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **B-frames:** 2 (improves quality; harmless since CS2 leaves GPU spare)
- **Look-ahead / Psycho Visual Tuning:** On for cleaner smokes

Since CS2 runs light on the GPU at competitive settings, NVENC has plenty of room even on a mid-range card. On an RTX 40-series GPU, [AV1 to YouTube or Kick](/blog/nvenc-av1-explained/) squeezes noticeably more quality from the same bitrate.

### x264 (only with a spare CPU)

Software encoding is risky in CS2 — it competes with the game for the CPU. Use it only if you have a high core-count chip you're not fully utilizing, and keep the preset light.

- **Preset:** Fast or Faster (never Medium while gaming — it will drop game frames)
- **Profile:** High
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds

## In-Game Settings

Competitive CS2 is about visibility and frames, but a few settings also help your stream compress cleanly:

- **Global Shadow Quality:** Medium
- **Model / Texture Detail:** Medium or High
- **Shader Detail:** Medium
- **Particle Detail:** Low
- **Ambient Occlusion:** Off
- **High Dynamic Range:** Performance
- **FidelityFX Super Resolution:** Off (upscaling adds blur that looks bad on stream)
- **MSAA:** 4x or 8x — anti-aliasing reduces shimmering edges, which actually helps the encoder
- **Texture Filtering:** Bilinear (minimal FPS cost)
- **Motion Blur:** Off (blur wastes bitrate and hurts clarity)

## Audio Settings

- **Track Bitrate:** 160 Kbps AAC — footstep and callout clarity matters in CS2
- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz
- **Mic Processing:** Noise Suppression + Compressor; add a Noise Gate to cut mechanical keyboard clatter
- **Separate Tracks:** keep mic, game, and Discord on distinct tracks for clean VODs

## Performance and Platform Tips

1. **Use NVENC** — the single most important choice for a CPU-bound game like CS2.
2. Cap FPS above your monitor refresh (e.g. `fps_max 400`) to smooth frame pacing without uncapped spikes.
3. Add the `-high` launch option for elevated process priority.
4. Disable the Steam and Discord overlays to avoid frame-time hitches.
5. Set OBS to **Game Capture** for the lowest overhead.
6. If you see dropped frames, work through the [dropped frames in OBS fix](/blog/how-to-fix-dropped-frames-in-obs/); if OBS reports "Encoding overloaded," see the [encoding overloaded guide](/blog/how-to-fix-encoding-overloaded-obs/).

## FAQ

### Should I stream CS2 in 720p or 1080p?

If your PC or upload can't reliably sustain 1080p 60fps, drop to 720p 60fps rather than 1080p 30fps. In a reaction shooter, smooth motion beats extra pixels.

### Why does my CS2 stream tank my FPS?

Almost always because you're using x264 software encoding, which fights CS2 for the CPU. Switch to NVENC and your in-game frame rate should recover immediately.

### What bitrate for CS2 smokes on Twitch?

Use the full 6,000 Kbps in CBR. Smokes are the hardest thing to encode in CS2, and there's no benefit to leaving bitrate on the table at Twitch's cap.

## Bottom Line

CS2 is defined by its CPU appetite, so the winning formula is simple: NVENC at 1080p 60fps, CBR at the platform's ceiling (6,000 on Twitch, more on YouTube/Kick), 2-second keyframes, and in-game settings tuned for frames and visibility.

For streaming Genshin Impact, **4,500–6,000 Kbps at 1080p 60fps** is the sweet spot on most platforms, and you can often drop to **3,500–4,500 Kbps** without noticeable quality loss. Genshin's anime-style art direction — wide flat color fields, clean cel-shading, minimal film grain — is one of the most encoder-friendly visual styles in gaming. That means you get better-looking streams at lower bitrates compared to hyper-realistic titles like Cyberpunk 2077 or Call of Duty. For recording locally, CQP 20 with NVENC HEVC will give you near-perfect quality at around 2–3 GB per hour.

## Why Genshin Impact Compresses So Well

Most video encoders work by finding redundancy between frames and within flat regions of color. Genshin gives the encoder an enormous amount to work with:

- **Large flat color zones** — character skin, sky gradients, Teyvat's stylized terrain
- **Consistent cel-shaded outlines** — sharp, predictable edges that don't introduce noise
- **No film grain or post-processing noise** — unlike shooters, there's nothing randomizing the frame
- **Predictable camera movement** — exploration and combat follow smooth arcs, not chaotic micro-strafes

The practical result: Genshin at 4,500 Kbps on x264 Medium looks roughly as clean as a fast-paced FPS game at 6,000 Kbps. ### Encoder Choice: NVENC vs x264

**NVENC H.264 (recommended for most Genshin streamers):** If you have an NVIDIA GTX 1650, RTX 20/30/40-series GPU, use NVENC. Set quality preset to P5 (or "Quality" in Simple mode) and bitrate to 5,000–6,000 Kbps. Your CPU stays free for Genshin's open-world loading and particle effects, which can be surprisingly demanding.

**x264 (recommended if you have a powerful CPU or a dedicated streaming PC):** x264 Medium at 4,500–5,500 Kbps will edge out NVENC H.264 at the same bitrate on Genshin specifically — because flat color content benefits more from x264's superior motion estimation. Use "Medium" or "Fast" preset, never "Veryfast" unless your CPU is genuinely struggling.

**NVENC AV1 (RTX 40-series only):** If your GPU supports it and you're streaming to YouTube or Kick, AV1 at 3,500–4,500 Kbps matches or beats standard H.264 at 6,000 Kbps for Genshin. The efficiency gain is dramatic on flat-color anime content. Read more in the [CBR vs VBR for streaming guide](/blog/cbr-vs-vbr-for-streaming/).

## In-Game Genshin Settings That Affect OBS Quality

Your in-game settings directly influence what OBS has to capture and encode. A few settings matter more than others:

- **Render Resolution:** Set to 1.0 (100%) and match your OBS canvas resolution. Upscaling from a lower render resolution inside Genshin creates artifacts OBS then has to encode.
- **Anti-Aliasing:** SMAA or FSR at Quality look clean in recordings; TAA with sharpening can introduce temporal flickering that increases bitrate demands slightly.
- **Motion Blur:** Turn it off for streaming. Motion blur is already encoded into the video signal by the encoder — adding in-game blur on top creates more encoder work with no viewer benefit.
- **VSync / FPS Limit:** Cap at 60fps in-game. An uncapped framerate wastes GPU cycles and can cause OBS frame timing issues. Match your OBS FPS target exactly.
- **Shadow Quality:** Has minimal effect on streaming quality but impacts GPU load. If you're on a single-PC setup and see frame drops during Dragonspine or Inazuma area loads, drop shadows to Medium.

## Quick Troubleshooting

**Stream looks pixelated during Dragonspine / snowstorm effects:** Particle systems and environmental effects (snow, rain, Electro storms) temporarily spike encoding complexity. Either raise your bitrate by 500–1,000 Kbps, or switch from NVENC H.264 to x264 Medium which handles sudden complexity spikes more gracefully.

**CPU spikes in Inazuma or Fontaine:** These regions have dense environmental geometry. Use NVENC instead of x264 to free CPU headroom. In OBS, go to Settings → Output → Encoder and switch to NVENC H.264.

**OBS shows "High Encoding" warning:** Lower the x264 preset from Medium to Fast, or drop resolution from 1080p to 900p (1600x900). Genshin's art style still looks excellent at 900p because clean lines scale down gracefully.

**Dropped frames (network):** Lower bitrate in 500 Kbps increments. Because Genshin compresses so well, even 3,500 Kbps at 1080p 60fps is watchable — you have more flexibility here than with other games.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What bitrate should I use for Genshin Impact on Twitch?

For Twitch at 1080p 60fps, use **5,000–6,000 Kbps**. Because Genshin's flat anime colors compress very efficiently, 5,000 Kbps looks nearly identical to 6,000 Kbps in practice. If your upload speed is limited, you can go as low as 3,500–4,000 Kbps at 1080p and the stream will still look clean during most gameplay.

### Does Genshin Impact run well on a single-PC streaming setup?

Yes, better than most open-world games. Genshin's GPU requirements are moderate compared to AAA titles, which leaves headroom for NVENC encoding without frame drops. On a mid-range single-PC setup (RTX 3060 or better, Ryzen 5 5600 or better), you can stream at 1080p 60fps with NVENC H.264 at 5,000–6,000 Kbps without impacting in-game performance. Avoid x264 on a single-PC setup unless you have a high-core-count CPU.

### What resolution should I record Genshin at for YouTube uploads?

Record at your native monitor resolution — 1080p, 1440p, or 4K. Genshin's art style genuinely benefits from higher resolutions because the clean lines and vibrant color work don't lose detail the way photorealistic games do. A 1440p 60fps recording at CQP 20 takes up roughly 5–8 GB per hour and looks exceptional on YouTube. Check storage needs with the [Recording Time Calculator](/tools/recording-time-calculator/).

### Should I use H.264, HEVC, or AV1 for Genshin streaming?

For **streaming:** H.264 (NVENC or x264) is the safe default since all platforms support it. If you have an RTX 40-series GPU and stream to YouTube, try AV1 — Genshin's flat colors benefit enormously from AV1's efficiency, letting you match H.264 quality at nearly half the bitrate. For **recording:** HEVC (H.265) via NVENC is the best choice for most users — roughly 40% smaller files than H.264 at the same visual quality.

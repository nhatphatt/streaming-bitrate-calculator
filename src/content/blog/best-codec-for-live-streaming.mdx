Picking a codec is one of the few streaming decisions that touches everything: image quality, upload requirements, hardware load, and whether your viewers can even watch smoothly. This guide compares the three codecs that matter for live streaming today and tells you which to use in real situations.

## Quick Answer

**For most live streams in 2026, H.264 is still the safest default. Use AV1 only when both your GPU (RTX 40-series, Arc, or newer) and your platform support it end to end. HEVC is rarely worth it for live streaming because platform and browser playback support is inconsistent.**

The reason is simple: a codec only helps if the whole chain supports it — your encoder, the platform's [ingest server](/glossary/ingest-server/), and every viewer's device. H.264 wins on that chain today, even though it is the least efficient of the three.

## How These Codecs Actually Differ

A [codec](/glossary/codec/) determines how much visual quality you can squeeze into a given [bitrate](/glossary/bitrate/). Newer codecs pack more detail into fewer bits, which means either better quality at the same bitrate or the same quality at a lower bitrate.

| Codec | Relative efficiency | Live playback support | Encoder hardware |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| H.264 (AVC) | Baseline (1x) | Universal | Every GPU + CPU |
| HEVC (H.265) | ~30–40% better than H.264 | Patchy in browsers | Most GPUs since ~2015 |
| AV1 | ~30% better than H.264, royalty-free | Growing (YouTube, Twitch beta) | RTX 40+, Arc, RDNA3+ |

"Efficiency" is the whole story. If AV1 is ~30% more efficient, a 6,000 Kbps AV1 stream can look roughly like an 8,500 Kbps H.264 stream — a big deal when your upload is capped.

## H.264: The Universal Default

H.264 has been the backbone of live streaming for over a decade. Its strength is not quality per bit — it is that literally everything decodes it: phones, browsers, smart TVs, old laptops, consoles.

- **Best for:** Twitch, Facebook, Kick, and any audience you don't control.
- **Encoders:** x264 (CPU) at the Medium or Slow preset gives the best quality per bit; NVENC, AMD AMF, and Intel QuickSync offload the work to your GPU with minimal quality loss on modern hardware.
- **Weakness:** At low bitrates (fast-motion games under ~6,000 Kbps), H.264 shows blocking and smearing sooner than the newer codecs.

If you are unsure what to use, use H.264. You will never have a viewer who can't watch, and the [OBS](/glossary/obs/) defaults already assume it.

## HEVC / H.265: Good on Paper, Awkward for Live

HEVC compresses noticeably better than H.264 and is excellent for recording and file storage. For **live** streaming, though, it hits a wall: many browsers and older devices can't decode HEVC in a stream reliably, and platform support for HEVC ingest is limited compared to H.264 and AV1.

The result is that HEVC's efficiency advantage rarely reaches your audience live. It shines for local recording and archiving — see [how to reduce video file size](/blog/how-to-reduce-video-file-size/) — but for going live it is usually the wrong pick. If you're deciding between the newer codecs, our [H.264 vs H.265 vs AV1 breakdown](/blog/h264-vs-h265-vs-av1/) goes deeper.

## AV1: The Best Long-Term Choice

AV1 is royalty-free and roughly 30% more efficient than H.264. That efficiency is exactly what helps at the bitrates most platforms cap you at. On a Twitch-style ~6,000 Kbps limit, AV1 can deliver visibly cleaner 1080p60 for busy games than H.264 can.

### What you need to run AV1

- **Encoder hardware:** NVIDIA RTX 40-series or newer, Intel Arc, or AMD RDNA3+. [NVENC AV1 is explained here](/blog/nvenc-av1-explained/).
- **Platform support:** YouTube Live accepts AV1, and Twitch has rolled out AV1 ingest through its enhanced broadcasting path. Confirm your platform before committing.
- **Viewer decoding:** Because platforms transcode server-side, most viewers still receive an H.264 rendition — so viewer devices are usually not the blocker.

### When AV1 is worth it

AV1 makes the most sense at high resolutions and frame rates where bitrate is the limiting factor — 1440p and [4K streaming](/size/4k-streaming-bitrate/), or fast 1080p60 gameplay that struggles inside a hard bitrate cap. If your GPU supports it and your platform accepts it, AV1 is the clear upgrade path.

## How to Choose in Practice

1. **Streaming to Twitch/Kick/Facebook on older hardware?** Use H.264 with x264 (Medium) or NVENC.
2. **Have an RTX 40+/Arc GPU and stream to YouTube or Twitch enhanced broadcasting?** Try AV1 and lower your bitrate slightly for the same quality.
3. **Recording locally, not going live?** HEVC or AV1 both save major storage versus H.264.
4. **Mixed or unknown audience?** Stay on H.264 — compatibility beats efficiency for live.

## FAQ

### Is AV1 better than H.264 for streaming?

In pure efficiency, yes — AV1 delivers roughly 30% better quality per bit. But it only helps if your GPU can encode it and your platform accepts it. When both are true, AV1 is the better choice, especially at high resolutions.

### Why not just use HEVC for live streaming?

HEVC compresses well but has weaker live playback and ingest support than H.264 and AV1. It's excellent for recording and archiving, but for going live it usually isn't worth the compatibility risk.

### Does changing codec reduce my upload requirement?

Yes. A more efficient codec lets you hit the same visual quality at a lower bitrate, which directly lowers your required [upload speed](/blog/how-much-upload-speed-to-stream-1080p-60fps/). That's the main practical reason to move to AV1.

## Bottom Line

H.264 remains the dependable default because everything supports it. AV1 is the future and already worth using if you have an RTX 40-series, Arc, or RDNA3+ GPU and a platform that accepts it. HEVC stays in the recording toolbox, not the live one. Match the codec to your hardware and platform, set a sensible bitrate, and you'll get the cleanest stream your upload allows.

Apex Legends packs slides, wall-jumps, ziplines, and stacked ability effects into a fast, large-map battle royale. All that motion and detail makes it one of the tougher games to compress cleanly — your OBS settings determine whether final-ring fights stay sharp.

## Quick Answer

**Stream Apex at 1080p 60fps with the full 6,000 Kbps CBR on Twitch (9,000–11,000 on YouTube), using NVENC to protect both CPU and GPU. Keyframe interval 2 seconds, High profile, and cap in-game FPS at 144 to avoid frame-time spikes that make the stream stutter.** Apex is demanding on both the CPU and GPU, so hardware encoding is the safe default.

## Recommended Resolution and FPS

Apex's constant movement — sliding down hills, tap-strafing, riding ziplines — means smooth frame delivery is critical. 60fps is non-negotiable; 30fps turns fast rotations and gunfights into a stuttery blur.

- **1080p 60fps** — the standard for a competitive battle royale stream.
- **720p 60fps** — a solid fallback for lower-end PCs or limited upload; keeps motion fluid.
- **1440p 60fps** — YouTube-only, where higher bitrate can actually feed the extra resolution. Twitch's cap leaves 1440p soft during chaotic fights.

In a fast BR, prioritizing frame rate over resolution keeps rotations and firefights readable. The [30fps vs 60fps streaming](/blog/30fps-vs-60fps-streaming/) article covers why smoothness wins in high-motion games.

## Bitrate by Platform

Apex's detailed environments and layered ability VFX give the encoder a lot to chew on, so run bitrate at the platform ceiling.

| Platform | Resolution | FPS | Bitrate | Encoder |
|----------|-----------|-----|---------|---------|
| Twitch | 1080p | 60 | 6,000 Kbps | NVENC P4–P5 |
| Twitch | 720p | 60 | 4,500 Kbps | NVENC P5 |
| YouTube | 1080p | 60 | 9,000–11,000 Kbps | NVENC P4 |
| YouTube | 1440p | 60 | 14,000–16,000 Kbps | NVENC P4 |
| Kick | 1080p | 60 | 7,000–8,000 Kbps | NVENC P4 |

At Twitch's 6,000 Kbps cap in CBR, most of the match looks good, but heavy ability usage in a stacked final ring — Bangalore smoke over Caustic gas over a Seer scan — may show minor artifacts. That's the ceiling at work, not a misconfiguration. [YouTube](/platforms/youtube/) and [Kick](/platforms/kick/) allow much higher bitrate and keep those moments clean; see the [twitch streaming bitrate guide](/blog/twitch-streaming-bitrate-guide/) for details.

## Why Apex Legends Is Demanding on Both CPU and GPU

Apex is unusual in taxing both major components, which is why hardware encoding is the sensible choice:

- **CPU-heavy game logic** — the Source-derived engine handles physics, movement tech, and 60-player-lobby simulation on the CPU, and Apex is notorious for CPU load in busy fights.
- **GPU-heavy rendering** — detailed maps, foliage, and ability particles push the graphics card, especially at high frame rates.
- **High-motion frames** — sliding, ziplining, and camera swings mean each frame differs greatly from the last, weakening [inter-frame compression](/glossary/encoding/) and demanding bitrate.

Because both the CPU and GPU are already working, x264 software encoding risks dropping game frames. NVENC sidesteps that by using the GPU's dedicated encoder block. The [NVENC vs x264 guide](/blog/nvenc-vs-x264-streaming/) explains the tradeoff.

## Encoder Settings

### NVENC (recommended)

The dedicated encoder chip runs alongside rendering, so you keep both CPU and GPU free for the game itself.

- **Rate Control:** CBR
- **Bitrate:** 6,000 Kbps (Twitch) / 9,000–11,000 Kbps (YouTube)
- **Preset:** P4 (Medium); step to P5 only if your GPU has clear headroom
- **Profile:** High
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds
- **B-frames:** 2
- **Psycho Visual Tuning:** On for busier fights

On an RTX 40-series GPU, switching to [AV1](/blog/nvenc-av1-explained/) for YouTube or Kick is a meaningful upgrade — around 30% more efficient, which directly helps Apex's high-detail scenes.

### x264 (use cautiously)

Because Apex is CPU-intensive for game logic, software encoding competes for cycles you can't spare in a fight. If you must use it, keep the preset light.

- **Preset:** Faster (never Medium while playing)
- **Profile:** High
- **Keyframe Interval:** 2 seconds

## In-Game Settings

These settings balance frames, visibility, and encoder-friendliness. Trimming effects and shadows reduces both GPU load and hard-to-compress detail:

- **Anti-Aliasing:** TSAA (smooths edges, reducing shimmer that wastes bitrate)
- **Texture Streaming Budget:** match your VRAM (4 GB / 6 GB / 8 GB)
- **Texture Filtering:** Anisotropic 4x
- **Ambient Occlusion:** Low
- **Sun Shadow Coverage / Detail:** Low
- **Spot Shadow Detail:** Off
- **Model Detail:** Medium
- **Effects Detail:** Medium (Low if you need more FPS)
- **Impact Marks:** Low
- **Ragdolls:** Low

Lowering **Effects Detail** and shadows is the highest-value change — it lifts your frame rate and cuts the particle complexity that spikes encoder load in fights.

## Audio Settings

- **Track Bitrate:** 160 Kbps AAC — footsteps and pings are competitive information in Apex
- **Sample Rate:** 48 kHz
- **Mic Processing:** Noise Suppression (RNNoise) + Compressor; add a Noise Gate for mechanical keyboards
- **Separate Tracks:** keep mic, game, and Discord on separate tracks for clean, monetization-safe VODs

## Performance and Platform Tips

1. **Cap FPS at 144** — uncapped Apex causes frame-time spikes that translate into visible stream stutter. Use the in-game limiter or `+fps_max 144`.
2. Use **Game Capture** in OBS, not Display Capture, for lower overhead.
3. Disable Steam and Discord overlays to avoid hitches.
4. Run Apex in **Fullscreen** (not Borderless) for the best performance and lowest latency.
5. **Twitch:** stick to 6,000 Kbps CBR with 2-second keyframes.
6. If the stream stutters, work through the [stream lag and stutter fix](/blog/how-to-fix-stream-lag-and-stutter/); for skipped frames, see the [dropped frames in OBS guide](/blog/how-to-fix-dropped-frames-in-obs/).

## FAQ

### Why does my Apex stream stutter even at high FPS?

Usually uncapped in-game frame rate. Wild FPS swings create uneven frame times that OBS can't smooth out. Cap at 144 (or your monitor refresh) to stabilize pacing.

### Should I use NVENC or x264 for Apex?

NVENC. Apex loads both CPU and GPU, so software encoding tends to drop game frames in fights. The dedicated encoder keeps your gameplay and stream smooth.

### What causes artifacts in Apex final rings?

Stacked ability effects — smoke, gas, and scans layered together — overwhelm Twitch's 6,000 Kbps cap. Use the full bitrate in CBR, lower Effects Detail to Medium, or stream to YouTube for more headroom.

## Bottom Line

Apex Legends is demanding on both CPU and GPU and full of high-motion, effect-heavy scenes, so let the GPU's NVENC block do the encoding. Lock in 1080p 60fps, CBR at the platform ceiling, NVENC P4 with 2-second keyframes, cap FPS at 144, and trim effects and shadows to keep fights clean.

Audio quality matters more than most streamers expect. Viewers will tolerate a 720p video feed, but harsh or noisy audio makes them click away almost instantly. This guide focuses on the choices that decide how you sound — connection type, capsule type, and pickup pattern — rather than chasing specific model prices.

## Quick Answer

**For most streamers, a good USB dynamic microphone on a boom arm is the best value: dynamic capsules reject room noise, and USB keeps setup simple.** Move to XLR only when you want an audio interface, more processing control, and room to grow.

## The Two Decisions That Matter Most

Almost every microphone question comes down to two choices: how it connects (USB vs XLR) and how it captures sound (dynamic vs condenser).

| Choice | Simpler / cheaper | More flexible / better isolation |
|--------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Connection | USB (plug and play) | XLR (needs an interface) |
| Capsule | Condenser (detailed, sensitive) | Dynamic (rejects room noise) |

### USB vs XLR

- **USB microphones** have a built-in audio interface, so they plug straight into your PC. No extra hardware, no gain-staging headaches. Ideal for most streamers and anyone with an untreated room.
- **XLR microphones** require a separate audio interface or mixer, adding cost and setup. In return you get more control over gain, EQ, and processing, easy upgrades, and pro-grade durability. Best for serious streamers and podcasters.

Some modern mics offer **both USB and XLR** in one body, letting you start on USB and move to an interface later without buying a new mic.

### Dynamic vs Condenser

- **Dynamic mics** are less sensitive by design, so they largely ignore keyboard clatter, PC fans, and room echo. That makes them the safer pick for typical bedroom or untreated setups — which is why so many streamers use them.
- **Condenser mics** are more sensitive and capture more detail and "air," but they also pick up *everything*, including background noise. They shine in a quiet, treated space with a shock mount and pop filter.

For most home streamers, a dynamic mic close to the mouth sounds cleaner than a condenser in the same room.

## Pickup Pattern

Nearly all streaming mics should be set to a **cardioid** pattern, which captures sound from the front and rejects the sides and rear — your voice in, room out. Multi-pattern mics also offer omnidirectional (all directions) and bidirectional (front and back, for two-person interviews) modes, but for solo streaming, cardioid is what you want.

## Microphone Categories by Budget

Rather than quote prices that change constantly, match your situation to a tier. Well-known lines from **Shure**, **Audio-Technica**, **Elgato**, **Fifine**, and **Rode** field options across all of these:

- **Entry USB** — an affordable USB cardioid mic. A big upgrade over any headset or webcam mic and enough for a new streamer to sound clear.
- **Mainstream USB** — a step up in build and capsule quality, often with onboard headphone monitoring and gain controls. The sweet spot for most streamers.
- **Streamer-focused USB with software** — USB mics paired with routing/processing software for mixing game, chat, and mic audio. Convenient if you want an all-in-one audio ecosystem.
- **USB/XLR hybrid** — one mic, both connections, often with built-in DSP. Great if you want a clear upgrade path.
- **Broadcast dynamic (XLR)** — the classic path for serious streamers and podcasters. Pair a broadcast dynamic mic with a quality interface; note that low-output dynamics may need extra clean gain (an inline preamp) to shine.

## OBS Audio Settings for Any Mic

Great gear still needs correct settings. Configure these in OBS regardless of which mic you own:

- **Sample rate:** 48 kHz
- **Audio bitrate:** 128–160 Kbps (AAC)
- **Noise suppression:** RNNoise (built into OBS) — effective AI-based noise removal
- **Noise gate:** set the threshold just above your room's ambient noise so silence stays silent
- **Compressor:** a gentle ratio (around 3:1) evens out volume spikes so you're not clipping on laughs and quiet on asides

These filters do more for perceived quality than a mic upgrade in many rooms. If your mic and video ever drift out of alignment, see [how to fix audio sync issues in OBS](/blog/how-to-fix-audio-sync-issues-obs/).

## Accessories Worth Getting

- **Boom arm** — positions the mic close to your mouth (better tone, less room) and keeps it off the desk to avoid vibration noise.
- **Pop filter** — tames plosives (P, B, T). Many arms include one.
- **Shock mount** — isolates the mic from desk bumps; close to essential for sensitive condensers.

## How Audio Bitrate Affects Your Stream

Audio bitrate adds to your total outbound bitrate. At 128 Kbps of audio plus 6,000 Kbps of video, your total is 6,128 Kbps — small, but it counts against your upload budget. ## FAQ

### Should a beginner get USB or XLR?

USB. It's cheaper, plug-and-play, and sounds excellent for streaming. XLR is worth it later when you want an interface and more processing control.

### Is a dynamic or condenser mic better for streaming?

For most home setups, dynamic — it rejects background noise so your room stays out of the stream. Condensers reward a quiet, treated space with more detail.

### Does a better mic matter more than a better camera?

Usually, yes. Clean audio keeps viewers watching far more than a resolution bump does. Get your audio right before spending on video upgrades.

## Bottom Line

Pick a **cardioid dynamic USB mic on a boom arm** and you'll cover the vast majority of streaming needs at a sensible price. Step up to a **USB/XLR hybrid** or a **broadcast dynamic on an interface** when you want more control and durability. Then spend ten minutes on your OBS filters — that combination beats an expensive mic in a noisy room every time. When your audio is dialed in, finish tuning your stream with the [bitrate tools](/tools/) and your [platform's settings](/platforms/twitch/).
